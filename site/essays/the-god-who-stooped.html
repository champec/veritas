The God Who Stooped

An essay on Leviticus, the sanctuary, and the meaning of sacrifice

There is a way of reading the Bible that turns God into someone you would not want to know.

It usually starts with the sacrificial system. A modern reader opens Leviticus, finds page after page of bulls and goats and lambs slaughtered at the altar, blood splashed on horns and curtains and ears, fat burned, kidneys consumed — and quietly concludes that whatever else this God is, He must want death. He must need it. Why else demand so much of it?

The picture that forms is hard to shake. A deity who cannot be approached without blood. Who counts His pardons in carcasses. Who installed a slaughterhouse at the centre of His people's worship and called it holy. By the time such a reader arrives at the cross, the framework is already set: God required a death, and finally He took one big enough to settle the account. Jesus was the last sacrifice because He was the biggest. The system was literal all along; He was just the climax of it.

It is a tidy reading. It is also, I think, almost completely wrong — and it makes God look exactly like the gods of every surrounding nation Israel was supposed to be different from.

To see why, we have to start somewhere unexpected: with what God actually said He wanted.

What God Said When He Was Asked Plainly

There is a verse in Jeremiah that ought to stop the whole conversation in its tracks. It comes in chapter 7, in the middle of God's most exasperated plea with His own people, and it is so direct that anyone reading the sacrificial system literally has to find a way to explain it away:

"For in the day that I brought them out of the land of Egypt, I did not speak to your fathers or command them concerning burnt offerings and sacrifices. But this command I gave them: 'Obey my voice, and I will be your God, and you shall be my people, and walk in all the way that I command you, that it may be well with you.'" (Jer 7:22-23)

Notice what He says He never asked for. Burnt offerings. Sacrifices. The very heart of the Levitical system. He is saying — under inspiration, in His own voice — that when He brought Israel out of Egypt, sacrifice was not the point. The point was relationship. Obey, listen, let Me be your God, let yourselves be My people, and we can do anything.

This is not a one-off. The same God says through Hosea:

"I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings." (Hos 6:6)

And through Micah, mocking the very logic of the literal reading:

"Will the Lord be pleased with thousands of rams, with ten thousands of rivers of oil? Shall I give my firstborn for my transgression, the fruit of my body for the sin of my soul? He has shown you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God?" (Mic 6:7-8)

And through David, who knew the system from the inside:

"Sacrifice and offering you have not desired... burnt offering and sin offering you have not required. Then I said, 'Behold, I have come to do your will, O my God; your law is within my heart.'" (Ps 40:6-8)

And through Isaiah, who watched the leaders of Jerusalem fill the temple with smoke:

"What to me is the multitude of your sacrifices? says the Lord; I have had enough of burnt offerings of rams and the fat of fed beasts... Bring no more vain offerings; incense is an abomination to me." (Isa 1:11-13)

These are not stray verses tucked into corners. They are the consistent voice of the prophets — the people who knew God best — telling Israel, loudly and repeatedly, that the system they took as the heart of their religion was not what God had been after the whole time.

So what was the system, then? If God did not need the blood — and He said, plainly, that He did not — what were all those animals for?

A Picture for People Who Could Not Yet Hear Words

Here is where the larger pattern of Scripture begins to make sense. God meets people where they are. He always has.

The Bible is, from one angle, a record of God adapting His method to whatever generation He happens to be working with. When the slaves came out of Egypt — terrified, illiterate, traumatised by four hundred years of brutality, freshly emerged from a culture saturated with crocodile-gods and bull-gods and rituals of appeasement — He did not hand them a book of systematic theology. He could not. They would not have understood a word of it.

Instead He gave them thunder and lightning and smoke at Sinai, because that was the language they had been conditioned to recognise as divine. He gave them visible miracles, columns of cloud, manna on the ground every morning. He gave them a tabernacle they could touch, priests in coloured robes, music, incense, ceremony, a routine of festivals through the year. He gave them, in short, a visual religion — because that is what people in their condition could absorb.

And He gave them the sacrificial system.

Not because He needed it. Because they did.

Paul, centuries later, put a name to what God had been doing. He called it the paidagōgos — the household slave whose job it was to walk children to school and home again, making sure they did not wander off into the bushes (Gal 3:24). The law, including all its ceremonies, was the paidagogue. It was never the teacher. The teacher was waiting at the school. The paidagogue's only job was to get the children there safely.

Read with that in view, the whole system suddenly snaps into shape. The lamb at the altar was not a magical transaction; it was a picture. The blood was not a payment; it was a visual aid. The priest was not a mediator God needed; he was a mediator the people needed, because they could not yet imagine going straight to God themselves. The whole apparatus was a kind of pedagogy in colour and sound and smell, designed to teach one truth over and over and over: sin destroys, and God provides a way out of it at His own cost.

The lessons inside the system, once you read them this way, are stunning. A man brought his lamb. He laid his hand on its head. He confessed his sin into its ears. He saw the animal die in his place. He watched the priest take the blood toward the most holy place. He went home knowing that what had just happened in miniature had a meaning he could not yet fully grasp — but the picture would stay with him. The whole thing was teaching him that sin really does cost a life, that God really does take it on Himself, and that he was loved enough to be spared. None of that required God to actually want the animal dead. The point was the lesson.

This is why the sacrificial system can be everywhere in the law and the prophets can still say God never wanted it. Both are true. He never wanted it. He gave it, because His people would not yet accept anything subtler.

The Same Pattern Everywhere Else

Once you see this once, you start seeing it everywhere in Scripture. God is constantly stooping to use methods He does not Himself prefer.

Consider the monarchy. When Israel demanded a king "like all the nations have," God told Samuel plainly: they have rejected Me from being king over them (1 Sam 8:7). He would much rather have led them directly. But they could not yet imagine it — they wanted someone they could see on a throne — so He gave them what they asked for and worked with the arrangement, even though it was never the ideal.

Consider divorce. When the Pharisees came to Jesus asking about it, He told them: because of your hardness of heart Moses allowed you to divorce your wives, but from the beginning it was not so (Matt 19:8). God had regulated a practice He did not prefer because the alternative — refusing to engage with His people's brokenness — would have left them worse off.

Consider Sinai itself. The people stood at the foot of the mountain and were terrified by the thunder. Moses had to tell them: do not be afraid (Ex 20:20). The terror was not what God wanted. He spoke that way because the audience needed it. Later He whispered to Elijah on the same mountain — a still small voice (1 Kings 19:12) — because Elijah did not need thunder. Different method, same God.

Consider Jesus stooping to spit on a blind man's eyes at Bethsaida (Mark 8:22-26). The spit was not magical. It was a tangible point of contact for a man whose faith needed something to hold onto. Even the second touch — I see men, but they look like trees — shows God meeting a person at the speed they can manage, not the speed He could deliver.

The whole Bible is this. Methods that are not His preference, used because His people are not yet able to receive His preference. The sacrificial system is one example among many.

What Happens When You Mistake the Method for the Man

The problem is that we keep doing the same thing the Israelites did — taking God's stooping personally and reading His character off it.

If you treat the thunder at Sinai as a revelation of who God is, you end up with a terrifying deity who likes to make a scene. But the thunder was for the audience. Moses knew Him as the friend who spoke face to face like a man with his friend (Ex 33:11).

If you treat the demand for a king as evidence that God favours monarchy, you have missed the chapter where He said it grieved Him to give them one. The monarchy was an accommodation. He preferred to be their king Himself.

If you treat the divorce law as evidence that God is comfortable with broken marriages, you have missed Jesus saying it was never the design. The regulation was damage control. He preferred something better.

And if you treat the sacrificial system as evidence that God needs blood, you have missed Jeremiah, Hosea, Micah, Isaiah, David, and the entire prophetic tradition saying He never asked for it in the first place. The system was a teaching device. He preferred — and said so loudly — a relationship in which we knew Him well enough not to need it.

Read literally, the system makes God look bloodthirsty. Read literally, the thunder makes Him look terrifying. Read literally, the monarchy makes Him look like He wanted what He never wanted, and the divorce law makes Him look like He approved of what He never approved.

Each of these is the same mistake. The audience-accommodation gets mistaken for the character of God. The slave-walking-the-children-to-school gets mistaken for the teacher. The picture gets mistaken for the person.

The Cross, Reread

This brings us, inevitably, to the cross — because the literal reading of the sacrificial system has its biggest payoff at Calvary. If sacrifice was always literally required, then Jesus' death was the final, biggest payment. The Father needed blood; the Son provided it; the account was settled.

But everything we have just seen calls that reading into question. If God said I did not speak to your fathers about sacrifices, what does it mean to say the whole system was building toward one cosmic sacrifice He actually needed? If He said through Micah will the Lord be pleased with thousands of rams... shall I give my firstborn for my transgression? and answered with no — He has shown you what is good — then how can the cross be the moment He finally cashed in on the very logic Micah said was wrong?

A better reading is available, and it is more honest to the whole Bible. The cross was not the moment God finally got what He had always wanted. It was the moment God showed, in person, what He had been trying to communicate through the whole picture-system for two thousand years.

It was the demonstration the symbols had been pointing toward. The lamb had been pointing to Him — yes — but not as "the final transaction." As the final unveiling. The point of every animal dying in front of every confessing Israelite was: this is what sin does, and this is the kind of God who is willing to bear it Himself when it finally runs its course in front of Me. The cross was God stepping out from behind the visual aid and saying, in flesh and blood: here is what I have been showing you the whole time.

He did not need to do it to satisfy His own anger. He did it to settle ours. He did it to answer the universe's question about the kind of person He is. He did it because demonstration was finally possible — because, after thousands of years of pictures, He could afford to come and be the thing the pictures were of.

Read this way, the cross stops being a transaction and becomes a revelation. It stops looking like Nebuchadnezzar — worship me or I'll burn you — and starts looking like Jesus — no greater love has anyone than this, that he lay down his life for his friends. It stops painting God as the one who needed blood, and starts painting Him as the one who was always willing to bleed.

A God Worth Knowing

There is a final thing to say, and it is the heart of why this matters.

If we read the sacrificial system literally — and the cross with it — we end up with a God who is, frankly, hard to love. Petty. Demanding. Strict about ritual. Needing to be paid before He can forgive. Such a God can be feared. He can be served. He cannot easily be admired, and he certainly cannot be enjoyed.

If we read the system the way God Himself described it — as teaching, as accommodation, as a stooping to meet hard-hearted people where they were — a very different person emerges. Someone patient enough to spend two thousand years drawing pictures because His audience could not yet hear words. Someone humble enough to use methods He did not prefer, because His people needed them. Someone honest enough, through His prophets, to keep saying I never wanted this — I want you. Someone who, when the moment finally came to step out from behind the symbols, did not appear in the form of a king or a judge or an avenging fire, but as a Galilean carpenter who washed His friends' feet and forgave the men driving the nails through His hands.

That is a God you can admire. That is a God you can want to spend eternity with.

And that, I suspect, is exactly the God the whole picture-system was trying to show us all along. The tragedy is that for centuries we have been so busy studying the pictures that we have missed the person they were drawn to introduce us to.

The lamb was never the point. The blood was never the point. The altar was never the point.

The point was always Him.
