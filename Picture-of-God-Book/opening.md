# Opening: The Campaign for Our Trust

Have you ever cast a vote in a political election? We are all familiar with how the process plays out: candidates campaign, they smear their opponents, and we are left to suss out who is telling the truth, who represents our deepest values, and who is worthy of our vote. 

God is the leader of the universe. But who voted for Him? Who elected Him, and who chose Him? 

Some would argue that God belongs in a completely separate category of leadership. He is the Creator, so infinite in power and majesty that earthly concepts of leadership contests simply do not apply. They believe His sovereignty is above our scrutiny, and that questioning Him is unfitting, even irreverent. 

While we can all agree that we wouldn't drag God into a human courtroom, the reality of the Bible is that His leadership and character *have* been questioned. This is not a matter of speculation; his right to rule was openly contested. What happens when an all-powerful Sovereign is challenged is not a mere theoretical question. It led to an open revolt in heaven, with one-third of the angels siding with the challenger, and it eventually reached our own planet, where our first parents were deceived into believing that God did not have their best interests at heart. 

What does a ruling tyrant do when his authority is questioned? We have seen the pattern repeat throughout human history: they silence the opposition by any means necessary. They control the press, dominate the narrative, and resort to total elimination if they cannot enforce compliance. They tolerate no questions, placing themselves far above the need to defend their actions. 

But for those who believe God responds like an earthly tyrant, there is surprising news: the Bible is actually the place where God lays out His case for our vote. Instead of staying aloof and demanding blind submission, He has voluntarily brought Himself to court so that His own created beings can judge Him. 

Even if you are righteous and telling the truth, once accused of wrongdoing, merely denying the allegations rings hollow. To win the trust of free, intelligent, reasoning beings, you must demonstrate the truth through visible evidence. 

What are the allegations God is responding to? While we do not possess a verbatim transcript of the original war in heaven, the biblical record allows us to reconstruct the charges. The attack on God’s character and His right to rule is laid bare in the opening pages of Genesis. The serpent (identified in Revelation as Satan) tells Eve that God is withholding her potential, that He has lied about the fatal consequences of disobedience, and that He is restrictive rather than generous. 

We see the same challenge in the Book of Job. Although readers often view the opening chapters as an attack on Job, it is actually an attack on God’s government. Satan charges that God is a poor judge of character who must buy the loyalty of His friends with material blessings. 

If God were a tyrant who refused to be questioned, why is Satan still alive? Why has God allowed His chief accuser's side of the story to be heard? Why bother addressing these allegations directly at all? 

The answer is that God is profoundly concerned about His reputation—He wants us to understand Him correctly. Throughout the Old Testament, God repeatedly states that He acts "for My name's sake"—not out of pride, but because some of His actions seem bizarre unless we realize they are done to demonstrate the truth about His character. 

His closest friends understood this. When God threatened to destroy Israel and start fresh, Moses didn't quietly submit; he challenged God on the basis of His reputation, saying, *"Think of how you will be perceived by the other nations you are trying to win!"* Abraham, another trusted friend, questioned God’s justice with equal candor: *"Will not the Judge of all the earth do what is right?"*

God operates this way because the only things He ultimately desires from us are love and trust—and these can only be freely given. Worship won by force is not worship at all; it is merely submission under duress, producing the character of a rebel. Love can only be won by convincing evidence and a demonstration of worthiness. This is why God has not simply destroyed the accuser or his followers; the charges must be allowed to play out and be shown to be completely false. As Paul writes in Romans, let God be proven true, even if every human is a liar, so that He may win His case when He is judged.

But is He actually worthy of our trust? 

That depends entirely on the kind of person He is. What kind of person would you want ruling the universe? Someone fair? Someone kind? Someone who selflessly puts others above Himself? Someone who remains constant during adversity, who is a masterful and honest communicator, and whom we have absolutely no reason to fear? 

The crisis in the heavenly family has given us a unique opportunity to watch how God manages this terrible situation. We get to watch Him guide a family that has been split by rebellion, ridicule, and rejection. We see exactly how He treats those who reject His love. 

At the end of this observation, the choice is yours: Is He worthy of your trust? Do you actually *like* the person He is? 

This is not an automatic outcome. Even when people see God for who He is, they are still free to reject Him. Indeed, many did exactly that when Jesus walked the earth. They saw His gentleness and humility and hated Him for it, ultimately crucifying Him. Humility and meekness are not everyone's cup of tea. When Moses was called the meekest man on earth, it wasn't a boast—meekness back then was often viewed as weakness, but Moses was simply owning who he was. 

Can we know God when we see Him for who He truly is? On the very character traits He has presented, and on the allegations He has faced—do we like Him? 

The evidence is woven throughout the entire Bible. In every book and every story, we find a lesson revealing what kind of person God is. Within these pages, we will systematically examine each book of the Bible, asking what His words, actions, and concessions reflect about His inner character. 

This approach of exploring the Bible is not new; I first learned of it from Dr. Graham Maxwell, who recorded a series of studies exploring this very theme. While you can visit his website and read his books, many people find it takes years of reading and rereading all 66 books to compile the most vital character lessons. 

I decided to do that work for you. By reading through the text, compiling relevant summaries, and extracting the key character principles book-by-book, this volume offers a clear glimpse into what I have learned. My hope is that it inspires you to return to the Bible yourself, armed with the same questions, ready to build upon the principles we uncover here.
