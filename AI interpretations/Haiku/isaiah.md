# Isaiah — God's Character

## Overview

Isaiah ministered for roughly sixty years, overlapping the Assyrian conquest of the northern tribes and the siege of Jerusalem. He is called the gospel prophet because his book carries one of the clearest portraits of the coming Messiah in the entire Old Testament. Read together, the two recordings show a consistent picture of what God is truly like — and what it cost when His people failed to know Him.

---

## 1. The Root of Israel's Failure Was a Wrong Picture of God

Isaiah opens with God's own diagnosis: *"The ox knows its owner, and the donkey its master's crib, but Israel does not know; my people do not understand"* (Isaiah 1:3). They were not less religious — by the time of Jesus they were more religious than ever, careful about tithing, Sabbath, and cleanliness. But their picture of God was wrong. Because it was wrong, they could not recognise God when He stood among them in person, and they called Him a heretic and killed Him. The prophets say it plainly: *"My people are destroyed for lack of knowledge"* (Hosea 4:6). The knowledge meant is not factual information — in Hebrew it is the same word used for the intimacy between husband and wife. They did not *know* God in the sense of loving trust and friendship.

---

## 2. God Answers Honest Questions With Evidence, Not Claims

Isaiah 61 describes what the Messiah will do: preach good news to the poor, bind up the broken-hearted, proclaim liberty to the captives. When John the Baptist, sitting in prison, sends his disciples to ask Jesus "Are you the one who is to come, or shall we look for another?", Jesus does not answer with a direct assertion. He says: *"Go and tell John what you hear and see — the blind receive their sight and the lame walk, lepers are cleansed and the deaf hear, and the dead are raised up"* (Matthew 11:4–5). He answers an honest question with evidence that fits the Isaiah description. The disciples spent the day with Him watching, then reported back. John drew his own conclusion. God does not require trust based on claims. He lets the evidence be examined.

---

## 3. Rote Religion Is an Insult to God

Isaiah 29:13: *"This people draws near with their mouth and honours me with their lips, while their hearts are far from me, and their fear of me is a commandment of men learned by rote."* God is not satisfied with mechanical compliance. Going through the forms of worship without genuine thought and engagement treats God as if He cannot tell the difference — as if He were no brighter than the gods served by spinning prayer wheels. The teacher's comment: the one who obeys God only from a sense of legal obligation "never enters into the joy of obedience — in fact he does not obey." God wants people who agree it is right, not people who follow because they were told to.

---

## 4. What God Most Desires Cannot Be Commanded

Isaiah 58:13 says to *"call the Sabbath a delight."* But delight cannot be ordered. God can ask people to rest; He cannot make them enjoy it. The passage is evidence of a remarkable thing about God's character: the very things He most desires from people — genuine love, real trust, authentic joy in His presence — are structurally beyond His ability to compel. He can only hope. The teacher says plainly: *"I cannot make you enjoy the Sabbath, but I hope you do."* A God who could command everything He wanted chooses, instead, to want things that can only be freely given.

---

## 5. God Exhausts Every Possibility Before Letting Go

The vineyard parable (Isaiah 5): God planted, cleared the stones, built a watchtower, hewed a wine vat, and planted choice vines. He waited and watched. What came back was wild grapes. Then God asks the question openly: *"What more was there to do for my vineyard that I have not done in it?"* This is God himself testifying in his own case that he left nothing undone. Before the discipline of captivity falls, He puts the question plainly — not defensively, but honestly — as someone who genuinely cannot think of what else could have been done.

---

## 6. God's Actions Are a Demonstration of the Truth About Himself

Isaiah 48:9: *"For my name's sake I defer my anger; for the sake of my praise I restrain it for you."* God acts with His own name — His character — in view. The teacher draws the larger point: the whole biblical record is God demonstrating the truth about Himself in the setting of the great controversy over His character and government. This is not self-serving. When God "wins" — when the truth about Him is vindicated — the whole universe wins, because every intelligent being needs to know whether the God at the centre of all things can be trusted. God is transparent about this motivation. He does not pretend otherwise.

---

## 7. God Appeals to Reason Against Idolatry

Isaiah 44 — a man cuts down a tree. He burns half to warm himself and bake bread. With the other half he makes a god, falls down before it, and says "Deliver me, for you are my god." Isaiah describes this plainly and lets the absurdity speak for itself. God does not simply forbid idolatry; He reasons against it. He treats His people as intelligent beings capable of seeing what is obvious when it is laid out clearly. This is the same God who says through Micah "Come, let us reason together" — a God confident that the truth about Him can withstand honest examination.

---

## Connections to Broader Concepts

- Israel's failure through a wrong picture of God supports the importance of Principle 10 (God wins trust by demonstration and evidence — a God who can only be known rightly through watching and listening, not through religious performance)
- The vineyard parable adds to Principle 9 (God goes to extreme lengths before letting anyone go)
- The John the Baptist story reinforces Principle 10 (God wins trust by demonstration and evidence)
- Isaiah 44 reinforces Principle 12 (God is not arbitrary — He appeals to reason)
- Isaiah 58:13 establishes the new Principle 14 (the things God values most cannot be won by force)
