# Obadiah, Jonah & Micah: God's Character Revealed

*(This recording covers Obadiah, Jonah, and Micah in two sessions.)*

## God Does Not Gloat Over the Destruction of the Wicked (Obadiah)

Obadiah is a whole book about gloating — and God's verdict against it.

- When Babylon destroyed Jerusalem, the Edomites (Israel's cousins, descended from Esau) stood by and gloated: "Tear it down! Raze it to its foundations!" (Psalm 137:7).
- Obadiah's message: "You should not have gloated over the day of your brother in the day of his misfortune" (v. 12).
- The principle is illustrated from family life: if one child is being disciplined, the others are not allowed to look pleased — gloating is treated as a higher crime than the original offense.
- The application: at the end of the millennium, when the wicked die, God's people will not gloat. "Could you get any satisfaction out of that?" God does not gloat when His children die — even the wicked are still His children.
- This connects to Romans 12:19 — "leave room for God's wrath." When we turn someone over to God, He might win them — and we may meet our enemy in the Kingdom.

**What this shows about God:** God does not gloat over the destruction of the wicked. They are still His children, and He watches them die with grief, not satisfaction. A God who gloated would be the kind of person Satan accused Him of being.

## God Feels the Pain of All His Children — and All Are His Children

God's love and connection to His children is deep and universal.

- God watches the sparrow fall and knows the number of hairs on our heads — illustrating His intimate, attentive love for every person.
- "When God numbers His children, does He only count the good ones?" We would never report our family that way — "I have five children... well, two are misbehaving tonight, so I only have three."
- God numbers them all. Even Lucifer is one of His children.
- That is why God will cry when the former Lucifer dies — the most brilliant of all His created beings, still His child, though a very bad one.
- Parents who never give up on wayward children reflect this: they pray for them, love to have them come home, and are glad they didn't hate them while they were gone.

**What this shows about God:** God never disowns His children, however badly they behave. He counts them all, loves them all, feels the pain of every one, and grieves over every one who is lost. We should be careful not to be too exclusive about who belongs to "the family of God."

## God Works for People Outside Israel — He Wants to Win Them All

Jonah and Nahum (both addressed to non-Jews, the Assyrians) are significant because most of the Bible follows one narrow line — the descendants of Abraham.

- God sent Jonah to Nineveh, Israel's enemy — a notoriously cruel people.
- Other examples of God working outside Israel: Rahab, Ruth, Naaman the Syrian, Cyrus, the message to Belshazzar, the Edomites.
- In the New Testament: Peter and Cornelius — Peter learned he was treating human beings like "creeping things."
- John 3:16 — "God so loved the world" — everybody in it.
- If the Bible contained all God has done everywhere, "there wouldn't be room in the world to hold all the books."

**What this shows about God:** God is not working only through one narrow channel. He is reaching all people everywhere. The Bible focuses on one line because that is where He most particularly dealt with problems that reveal the truth about Himself — but He wants to win them all.

---

## Jonah Knew God Too Well — and Was Embarrassed by It

Jonah is the mirror image of Jeremiah and Paul.

- Jonah's own description of God is one of the best in Scripture: "I knew that you are a gracious God and merciful, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love, and relent of evil" (Jonah 4:2).
- Jeremiah boasted in knowing God. Paul was proud of the good news about God's righteousness. **Jonah was ashamed.**
- Jonah ran away not because he doubted God's power, but because he knew God would forgive Nineveh — and that would make Jonah look like a false prophet.
- He cared more about his own reputation than about 120,000 people. "Your goodness embarrasses me to tears. It even angers me."
- The application: Adventists who predicted dates for the second coming and are now embarrassed by the delay. But the delay only confirms the truth about God — that He is gracious and forgiving, not willing that any should perish. The longer He waits, the better He looks.

**What this shows about God:** God's graciousness is so consistent that it can embarrass those who care more about their reputation than about people. The delay of the end is not an embarrassment to God — it is evidence of His character. We should be proud, not ashamed, of a God who waits.

---

## God's Warnings Are Conditional — He Relents When People Repent (Jonah)

- God said "40 days and Nineveh shall be overthrown." Nineveh repented — from the king down to the least — and God relented.
- "The promises and the threatenings of God are like conditional." This is not God changing His mind — it is God responding to genuine repentance, which is His consistent character.
- Jonah was angry that God relented. God was pleased.

**What this shows about God:** God's warnings are invitations to repent, not locked decrees. He is always ready to relent when people turn.

## God Does Not Want Sacrifices — He Wants Us (Micah 6:6–8)

Micah 6 is one of the clearest revelations of what God actually wants.

- "With what shall I come before the Lord? Burnt offerings? Thousands of rams? Ten thousand rivers of oil? My firstborn?" — hyperbole to make the point.
- "He has showed you, O man, what is good: to do justice, to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God." (Micah 6:8)
- God does not desire sacrifice — not even the sacrifice of His Son, *as a ritual*. He offered His Son to win us back, but if that death does not lead us to repentance and trust, it has failed as far as we are concerned.
- "Blood does not save — that's hematology. God saves. It costs the death of His Son to clarify everything that had to be clarified and to win us back to trust."
- The same is true of faith and the Bible: faith doesn't save, the book doesn't save — God saves, and He can't save people who don't trust Him.

**What this shows about God:** God does not want ritual, even the ritual of His Son's death. He wants us — reconciled, healed, walking humbly with Him. The death of Christ has meaning only if it draws us back to trust.

## The Prodigal Was Forgiven Before He Repented

The prodigal son is the ultimate statement of the plan of salvation.

- The son came home for half-good reasons: the food was better at home than in the pig pen.
- He planned a legal speech: "I will arise and go to my father... make me as one of your hired servants."
- The father had been looking for him for years. When he saw him — dirty, malnourished, diseased — he threw his arms around him "dirt and all" and said, "Son, I'm thrilled you've come home."
- When did the father forgive? When the son left home — when he helped him pack his bag and put extra money in his pocket. The son was forgiven before he repented.
- The son's repentance came *after* he realized he had been forgiven. "The kindness of God leads us to repentance" (Romans 2:4).
- Jesus on the cross: "Father, forgive them" — not "if they repent, I could forgive you." He forgave anyway. God is forgiveness personified.
- The older brother, concerned about legal matters and fairness, is the one who was in trouble.

**What this shows about God:** God forgives before we repent — His forgiveness is not conditional on our repentance. Our repentance is a *response* to His forgiveness. The legal model (confess to be forgiven) is an emergency measure for beginners; the ultimate truth is that God is forgiveness personified, and all we have to do is come home.

## Sin Is Wanting to Do Wrong — Not Just Breaking the Rules

- In the legal model, sin is breaking the rules. In the trust-healing model, sin is the untrusting rebel who's unwilling to listen — a matter of the heart.
- Paul and the tenth commandment: "Thou shalt not covet" means you shouldn't even *want* to do wrong. Paul realized he was a sinner not because he broke rules, but because he wanted to.
- "If sin is thought of as breaking the rules, you want forgiveness. If sin is seen as wanting to do these things, you want healing. That's the difference."
- The tenth commandment is the most spiritual: you can't force anybody to like it — they choose whether they want to or not. In the hereafter, neighbors won't just never come over to hurt — they won't even want to.

**What this shows about God:** God is not concerned merely with rule-breaking. He is concerned with the heart — with what we want. Salvation means healing, so that we don't even want to do wrong. This is why God's goal is a new heart and a right spirit, not mere legal acquittal.

---

## Salvation Means Healing, Not Legal Adjustment

- "To many, salvation means praying up your fire insurance and buying a ticket to the kingdom. The real meaning of salvation is healing the damage done."
- The legal model is for beginners — "pitched at the foot of Sinai, still scared of God." It is an emergency measure until we come to know God well.
- The mature view: "We are in trouble, and we are dying in our trouble. If He could only win us back to trust Him, He could readily heal the damage done."
- The legal model has been "curdling" the trust-healing model for a long time. The doctrine of hell (based on the immortality of the soul) has driven millions into preoccupation with their legal standing — because if you believe in eternal torment, all you care about is avoiding it.
- "Nothing has turned more millions against God than this belief" (attributed to the devil's teaching in Eden).

**What this shows about God:** God is not a legalistic deity adjusting our legal standing. He is a healer. Salvation means healing the damage done, and God can and will save and heal all who trust Him. The cross was not a legal transaction but a demonstration to win us back to trust.

---

## God's People Misunderstood the Ceremonial System

- The animal sacrifices were meant to be a constant reminder of sin and its cost — not a transaction to appease God.
- If Israel had understood the meaning of the animal sacrifices, they would have recognized Christ when He came.
- Instead, they turned it into a ceremony: "Now we've got better blood" — as if the blood itself had power.
- "Blood does not save — that's hematology. God saves." The same is true of the book (bibliology) and of faith — none of these save; God saves, and He can't save people who don't trust Him.
- Peter didn't understand the ceremonial system — "Far be it from you, Lord, don't talk about dying." David, from Psalm 51, would have understood at the cross.

**What this shows about God:** God gave the ceremonial system to teach salvation by trust in the Savior to come. Misunderstood, it became a curse. Properly understood, it would have prepared people to recognize Christ. The meaning, not the ritual, is what matters.

---

## God Will Not Force Even Lucifer — He Lets Go Only When There Is No Hope

- Lucifer prayed to be taken back. Jesus wept over him and said, "It's not that we're unwilling to forgive — we're forgiveness personified. But you have so long inhabited those habits of rebellious, illogical, and irresponsible thinking that you have destroyed both the capacity and the desire to respond to truth."
- Since truth spoken in love is God's only instrument of persuasion and healing, and Lucifer could no longer respond to it, there was no hope.
- The evidence the diagnosis was correct: when Satan heard it, he announced his intention to bring all of God's creation down in destruction — like Hitler when he knew he'd lost the war.
- God did not give him up at that moment — He had a long-range purpose in being misunderstood. Not until He let His own Son go did the universe see what "giving up" really means.

**What this shows about God:** God does not destroy even His worst enemy while there is any hope. He lets go only when the person has destroyed both the capacity and the desire to respond to truth — and even then, He weeps.

## Notes on What Is NOT Claimed

- The dating of Obadiah (9th century to post-exilic) is acknowledged as uncertain.
- The "great fish" of Jonah is accepted as historical (Jesus accepted it, Matthew 12:39–40), but the focus is on the message, not the mechanics.
- The similarity between Micah 4 and Isaiah 2 is noted but not treated as plagiarism — both may have drawn from a common source they loved to repeat.
- The discussion of legal vs. trust-healing models is acknowledged as candid and risky, but the recording insists on honesty rather than beating around the bush.
