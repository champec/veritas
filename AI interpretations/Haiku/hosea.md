# Hosea: God's Character Revealed

*(This recording covers the book of Hosea in two sessions.)*

## God's Wrath Is God Giving Up — Not God Lashing Out

This is the central theological contribution of Hosea — the recording explicitly flags it as the book's main purpose: "I believe it explains the Wrath of God."

- Hosea 4:17 — "Ephraim is wedded to idolatry; **let him alone**." The theme runs through the whole book.
- Hosea 11:8 — "How can I give you up, Ephraim? How can I hand you over, Israel? ... My heart recoils within me, all my compassion is kindled." God grieves as He lets go.
- Paul, a real student of Hosea, says the same in Romans 1:24, 26, 28 — **three times**: "God gave them up." This *is* God's wrath being revealed. The only thing God does to show wrath in Romans 1 is to give people up to their own choices.
- God doesn't beat them up, burn them, or impose penalties. He sadly turns away and walks home without His people — exactly what Hosea would have done if Gomer refused to come back.

**What this shows about God:** God's wrath is not retaliation or imposed punishment. It is the sorrowful act of letting go those who are bent on leaving Him. This is the definitive Old Testament demonstration of what "wrath" means.

---

## The Cross Is the One Time God Fully Gave Someone Up

Hosea raises the question: has God ever *completely* given someone up? The answer is the cross.

- Jesus was "made to be sin though he knew no sin" — treated as if He were a stubborn rebel bent on leaving the Father, though He never was.
- On the cross He cried, "My God, my God, why have you **forsaken me**?" — why have you given me up? He knew the meaning of it from Hosea.
- The cross is the one event in all eternity where God fully demonstrated what it means to give someone up — the death of a sinner — so that we could see what God's wrath really looks like.
- The proof Jesus was not actually a sinner: the Mount of Transfiguration, where He stood in the Father's unveiled glory and was not consumed.

**What this shows about God:** God did not ask us to believe a claim about His wrath; He demonstrated it once, on His own Son, so we could see what giving someone up actually costs Him.

---

## God Still Loves Those He Gives Up

"I can love them no more" (Hosea 11) does not mean God stops loving.

- The proof He still loves them is in the very next lines: "How can I give you up, Ephraim?"
- God loves everyone, but He can only *show* His love to those who make themselves available to receive it — like John, "the beloved disciple," who was always at Jesus' side.
- God will still love Lucifer as he perishes. He remembers the happy days when Lucifer bore light and truth. Lucifer will leave "an eternal void in the infinite memory of God."
- God will cry as the wicked die: "Why will you die?" (Ezekiel). He watches them perish still loving them.

**What this shows about God:** God's love never fails, even toward the unreachable. "Letting go" is never the failure of God's love — it is His love meeting a final refusal it will not override.

---

## God's Love Is Like a Faithful Husband (Hosea and Gomer)

The lived parable of Hosea and Gomer is the book's frame.

- God told Hosea to marry a faithless woman — mirroring God's relationship with Israel, who was unfaithful from the beginning.
- When Gomer left and ended up owned by a pimp, Hosea went looking for her, bought her back for the price of a maidservant (15 shekels + barley), and brought her home.
- But Hosea did **not** drag her home by the hair. He said, "I will wait for you" (Phillips). He wanted her to *want* to be home, not merely to be there.
- The parallel breaks down: Hosea got Gomer back, but God could not get Israel back. The ten tribes went into Assyria and oblivion; the two into Babylon.

**What this shows about God:** God is the faithful husband who pursues, buys back, and waits — but will not force. He wants His people to want to come home.

---

## You Cannot Force Love or Trust

The recording presses this repeatedly.

- "Can you force love? Can you terrify people into loving you? Does it work?"
- Hosea didn't threaten Gomer to make her come home. You don't say to someone "come home or I'll kill you" and expect them to come.
- Yet much Christian preaching implies exactly this: come home or God will burn you. "That has driven millions of people away from God, and the devil is behind it."
- Romans 2:4 — "the kindness of God leads you to repentance." No one has ever been won by a frown or by censure.
- Force produces even worse rebelliousness, and rebelliousness is the essence of sin.

**What this shows about God:** God will not use threats or force to win love, because love won by force isn't love. He wins only by kindness.

---

## To Know God Is to Love God

Hosea 6:6 (Hebrew parallelism) is key:

> "It is true love that I have wanted, not sacrifice;
> the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings."

- "True love" parallels "knowledge of God." To know God *is* to love Him — trust, admiration, intimacy.
- The devil knows more *about* God than anyone, but doesn't know Him in the biblical sense. Mere information is not salvation.
- "My people are dying for lack of knowledge" (Hosea 4:6) — not lack of facts, but lack of this knowing-love.
- Israel claimed "My God, we know you, we are your Israel" (8:2) while practicing idolatry — and Jesus later echoes: "I never knew you" (Matthew 7).

**What this shows about God:** God is not looking for people who know *about* Him. He is looking for friends who know Him in trust and love — the kind of knowing that changes the heart.

---

## God's People Can Be Religious Without Knowing Him

Israel kept the Sabbath, paid tithe, offered sacrifices, and claimed to be God's chosen — yet God said He never knew them.

- They watched the sun go down and cried, "When will the Sabbath be over, that we may buy and sell?" (preview of Amos) — yet they kept the day.
- They felt secure because they observed the right day and descended from Abraham.
- God wanted integrity, humility, and a willingness to listen — not the ceremonies.

**What this shows about God:** God is not impressed by correct religious performance. What He wants is honesty and a willingness to listen — the irreducible minimum.

---

## We Become Like What We Worship

Hosea 9:10 — "they became as abominable as the thing which they loved."

- This is "a law" repeated throughout Scripture: we become like the thing or person we worship and admire.
- Worship a crocodile, a beetle, a frog — and the mind darkens to that level. God doesn't have to darken it; the consequence is built in.
- This is why idolatry is so devastating — it is cause-and-effect, not arbitrary punishment.

**What this shows about God:** God's warnings about idolatry describe real cause-and-effect, not imposed penalties. A reasonable God produces reasonable warnings.

---

## God Treated Israel Gently Even While Disciplining Them

Hosea 11 is one of the tenderest passages in Scripture:

- "Yet it was I who taught Ephraim to walk, picking them up in my arms..."
- "I led them with gentle encouragement; their harness was a harness of love."
- "I bent down to them and gave them food" — even when they complained about the manna.
- Even when God "gave them up" to Assyria, it was after long, gentle reaching.

**What this shows about God:** God's discipline is never harsh or retaliatory. Even His corrections are carried out with the gentleness of a parent teaching a child to walk.

---

## Gossip Is a Serious Sin Because It Would Ruin the Hereafter

A striking aside in the Romans 1 list: gossiping appears "in the middle of the worst list of sins in the Bible."

- In the hereafter, memories will be better than ever. David will meet Uriah; they will become friends again — but only because both have been transformed.
- If one gossip were admitted, "there'd be no peace and comfort in the hereafter." A gossip with perfect memory and an audience would destroy the safety of the whole universe.
- This connects to the "safe to have around" theme from Daniel: God's concern is not mere forgiveness but whether a person is safe to admit.

**What this shows about God:** God takes character seriously because He is building a universe where people must be safe to live next door to. Sins that destroy trust and peace are not trivial to Him.

---

## Romans 4:25 Uses the Same Greek Word as Romans 1

A key linguistic connection that ties the cross directly to God's wrath as "giving up."

- Romans 4:25 says Christ was "delivered up" (paradidomi) for our offenses — the **exact same Greek word** used three times in Romans 1:24, 26, 28 for God giving people up.
- Most translations obscure this by rendering it "put to death" in 4:25 but "gave them up" in chapter 1 — even though it's the same verb.
- The Good News Bible is the most consistent: "given over" in both places.
- This ties the death of Christ directly to Romans 1: **God's wrath was poured out on Him, and He was given up.** That is the cause of His death.

**What this shows about God:** The cross is not a separate event from God's wrath — it *is* God's wrath, demonstrated once on His own Son. The same word describes what God does to rebels and what God did to Christ.

---

## The Cross Is God's Demonstration, Not a Claim

God did not ask the universe to take His word for what sin does and what His wrath looks like. He demonstrated it.

- The devil denied that sin results in death. God did not simply assert the truth; He came Himself and died that death, so the universe could see it.
- The devil caricatured God as "love me or I'll kill you." At the cross, God did not kill or torture His Son — He **gave Him up**. Jesus knew it: "My God, why have You given Me up?"
- The death that comes from sin is not torture and execution at the hands of a gracious God — it is the awesome consequence of being given up by One who cries as we go.

**What this shows about God:** God settles the truth about His character by open demonstration, not by claims or authority. The cross is the definitive demonstration of what God's wrath really is.

---

## Serving God from Fear Produces Enemies, Not Friends

- The obedience that springs from fear does not produce love or trust — and it destroys freedom.
- Those who obey the blueprint but don't know God as a friend may become His enemies even while defending the Sabbath.
- The ones who tortured Jesus to death were the most devout Sabbath-keeping, tithing, Bible-carrying people of their day. They served God from fear and had accepted the devil's caricature of Him. After nailing Him to the cross, they hurried home to keep the Sabbath holy.
- "Go away, I never knew you — we never were friends" — and it doesn't seem fair because we've worked hard, paid tithe, kept the Sabbath. But the issue was never performance; it was whether we knew Him.

**What this shows about God:** God is not looking for fearful obedience. Those who serve Him from fear — even while keeping the right day and the right rules — may become His enemies. What He wants is friends who know Him.

---

## God Is Not Two-Faced

- "To be loving is to be just, to be justice, to be righteous, to be righteousness, to be merciful — they're all the same thing, all the time."
- God does not have another face called "Justice" that is different from His face of love.
- God does not change in the end — He is the same at the end as He is now.

**What this shows about God:** God is not loving sometimes and just other times. Love and justice are the same thing in Him, all the time. He does not change.

---

## God Will Not Run a Penitentiary for Eternity

- God cannot keep cheats and liars in His Kingdom — unless He made part of the universe a penitentiary where He could keep them all separate so they couldn't do damage.
- God refuses: "I will not run a huge penitentiary for eternity. I will someday let all my cheating children go."
- This is why He gives them up — not out of anger, but because He will not preserve order by force forever.

**What this shows about God:** God will not maintain order by force or by eternal confinement. He would rather let the rebellious go than rule by coercion.

---

## The Giving Up Is More Awesome Than Anger

- "When somebody is angry, it inflames your own spine to resist. But when somebody who loves you — somebody you know would try again and again and again — when that person says 'there's nothing more I can do for you, I'm going to let you go,' you know that's final. You can't bargain with that."
- Some call this a weak picture of God. It is far more powerful. The infinitely loving One saying "you're joined to idols; we're going to have to let you go" — and then crying as we die — that is a much greater power than the picture of a God who thunders in anger.

**What this shows about God:** God's sorrowful letting go is more final and more powerful than anger. A God who grieves as He releases the rebellious is greater than a God who thunders.

---

## Notes on What Is NOT Claimed

- The recording notes the hazard of pushing illustrations too far (e.g., the rich man and Lazarus used to prove the immortal soul). The Hosea-Gomer parable should be taken only as far as Scripture itself takes it.
- The details of Hosea's marriage (whether Gomer was faithful at first, the identity of the children's fathers) are treated cautiously — the main point is God's relationship with Israel, not the domestic details.
- The "latter rain," "day of the Lord," and Daniel 12:4 connections are flagged as topics for Joel/Amos next time, not developed here.
