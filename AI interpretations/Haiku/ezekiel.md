# Ezekiel: God's Character Revealed

*(Background: Ezekiel was among the Jews carried to Babylon in Nebuchadnezzar's second invasion (around 593 BC), before Jerusalem's final destruction in 586. Daniel was already there; Jeremiah was still back in Jerusalem pleading with the people. So three prophets were at work at once—Jeremiah in the land, Daniel and Ezekiel in exile. Ezekiel's audience was a crushed, bewildered people: stranded far from the Promised Land, certain God had failed His covenant with Abraham, while the surrounding nations sneered that the God of Israel must be too weak to protect His own. The book is full of strange symbols—most famously the "wheels within the wheels"—and very strong language about Israel's sin. The central question of this study is not the schedule of events but what the book reveals about God.)*

---

## God Met Discouraged, Confused People With What They Needed (The Wheels Within the Wheels)

The book's most famous image—wheels within wheels that "can proceed without turning," with living creatures, eyes, and a throne above—is deliberately overwhelming and hard to decode. It has spawned centuries of speculation (one newspaper even claimed Ezekiel had seen a spaceship). But Ezekiel's own takeaway is simple: "such was the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the LORD," and he "fell upon his face," then was told, "Son of man, stand upon your feet"—and went out reassured.

The point for the captives was not a puzzle to solve but a reassurance to receive: their God had **not** lost control and had **not** been caught by surprise. He had even foretold the rise and fall of the nations, and would bring them home in precisely seventy years (just as Jeremiah said—not the two years the false prophets promised). To a people too discouraged and confused to follow a detailed argument, God gave exactly what they needed: a calm, overwhelming reminder of His presence and control, pitched to where they actually were.

**What this shows about God:**
- God does not rebuke His people for being confused or overwhelmed. He meets discouraged, half-understanding people with the reassurance they can receive, sympathizing with their limited grasp rather than demanding they first understand. *(Recorded as an Ezekiel entry under Principle 3, "God Meets Us Where We Are," in `broader-concepts.md`.)*

## God's Wrath Is Letting Go—and It Grieves Him (Ezekiel 18; 20; 33)

Ezekiel states plainly what happens to those who finally refuse God, and how God feels about it: "Why will you die, O house of Israel? For I have no pleasure in the death of anyone, says the Lord GOD; so turn, and live" (Ezek 18:31–32; cf. 33:11). This echoes Hosea's "How can I give you up?"

The crucial insight the book makes explicit: **in Scripture, "letting a person go" is the very language of God's wrath.** God's wrath is not Him lashing out or inflicting torment; it is His sorrowful release of those who insist on leaving. The rebel dies not because God strikes him down but because "you cannot live alone"—he has cut himself off from the only source of life. The kindness of God (Romans 2:4) does not change this: He remains kind, and still must let the unwilling go—"as He let His Son go." On the cross, Jesus "was given up" and cried, "My God, my God, why have You forsaken Me?"—tasting the death of the sinner, which is to be released.

**What this shows about God:**
- God does not destroy those who reject Him; He finally, grievingly lets them go, and that releasing—not active torment—is what Scripture calls His wrath. *(Recorded under Principle 8, "God Ultimately Lets Go Those Who Reject His Love," in `broader-concepts.md`.)*

## God Acts "For the Sake of My Name"—and Submits Himself to Judgment (Ezekiel 20)

When the elders came demanding why God had let them end up in Babylon, God reasoned through Israel's whole history (Ezek 20) with a refrain: "I acted for the sake of My name." He brought them out of Egypt, and would bring them back from exile, **not because they deserved it**—they did not—but to "say something true about Himself" before a watching universe ("we are a spectacle to angels and to men").

This is easy to misread as vanity—a God anxious about His image. The deeper point is the opposite. Because God wants people to trust and obey Him *freely*, on the strength of evidence rather than coercion, He pours everything into making the truth about His character plain, and then **lays that character open to examination**. No one drags Him into court; He presents Himself there willingly: "Taste and see that the LORD is good" (Ps 34:8); "that You may be justified in Your words, and prevail when You are judged" (Ps 51:4; Romans 3:4). The same posture runs from Eden—where He left the forbidden tree and the serpent's rival claim within reach of free choice—through Moses at the rock (where misrepresenting God was treated as the gravest fault), to Job. The friends of God understand and welcome this: Moses had once urged, "God, don't do something that would hurt Your reputation with the nations."

The greatest lesson is the sheer condescension of it: an all-powerful God who could simply command belief instead stoops to stand trial and wait for a free verdict.

**What this shows about God:**
- God invests everything in the truth about His character and then voluntarily submits Himself to scrutiny and judgment, so that our trust can be a free response to the evidence rather than something coerced. *(Recorded under Principle 10, "God Wins Trust by Demonstration and Evidence," in `broader-concepts.md`.)*

## Interpretation Notes

- **Scripture often says God "does" what He actually permits.** "I gave them statutes that were not good… making them offer by fire all their firstborn" (Ezek 20:25–26) does not mean God commanded child sacrifice (He calls it detestable); it is the same idiom as "God slew Saul" (a suicide) and "God hardened Pharaoh's heart"—God, as ruler, taking responsibility for what He permitted. *(Recorded in `biblical-interpretation.md` and `proper-context.md`.)*

- **Don't build doctrine on obscure symbols; start with the clear picture of Christ.** The wheels, the temple river, and similar images have produced endless speculation (spaceships; "Rosh" = Russia; "powers of the heavens" = uranium "split"). The safe method is to take the Bible as a whole, begin with the life of Christ, and only then venture into the symbols—never resting a doctrine on a detail whose meaning cannot be established. *(Recorded in `biblical-interpretation.md`.)*

## Difficult Passages (see `proper-context.md`)

- **Ezekiel 20:25–26** — God appearing to give harmful laws and to be behind child sacrifice (resolved via the "God did what He allowed" pattern).
- **Ezekiel 24:15–24** — God forbids Ezekiel to mourn his wife's death, as a living sign to Israel; like Job and Hosea, a friend of God asked to bear a painful thing to "say something" about Him.

---

## Summary

To a beaten, confused people, Ezekiel reveals a God who meets discouragement with reassurance rather than rebuke; whose "wrath" is the grieving release of those who will not stay, not active destruction; and who, far from defending His reputation out of vanity, lays His own character open to judgment so that we can freely decide on the evidence. The strange symbols and the strong language about sin all serve that picture: a God in no way arbitrary, who would rather stand trial than compel belief.
