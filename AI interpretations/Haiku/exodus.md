# Exodus: God's Character Revealed

## Mount Sinai: God's Terrifying Power and Majesty

God came down on Mount Sinai with thunder, lightning, earthquake, and thick smoke. The people trembled with fear.

**The People's Reaction:** "Don't let God speak to us or we'll die! Moses, you speak to us instead." They asked for someone in between.

**What This Shows:**
- God's infinite power and glory are so overwhelming that sinners cannot survive seeing His unveiled majesty
- Even though God loved these people and wanted to reveal Himself to them, He had to veil His dazzling glory so they wouldn't be consumed
- Fear and reverence were mixed together—the people confused terror with proper respect
- God did not want them to be terrified; He wanted them to understand who He is with reverence

**The Contrast:** Later, Jesus wept over Jerusalem, saying, "How I wanted to gather you together as a hen gathers her chickens." The people didn't recognize this as the same God who thundered on Mount Sinai. They said, "You don't sound like the God of Sinai. You're teaching us to turn the other cheek and pray for our enemies. You're contradicting the Old Testament."

**God's Dilemma:** Hard-hearted people coming from slavery in Egypt wouldn't listen to a still, small voice. They needed to be shocked into paying attention. God raised His voice because His people were hard of hearing—not because He wanted to terrify them, but because He had to get their attention first.

## Moses: God's Friend with No One In Between

Unlike the other Israelites, Moses spoke to God face to face, as a friend speaks to a friend. No priest, no intermediary, no barrier.

**What This Shows:**
- God's deepest desire is to treat people as friends, not servants
- God didn't require anyone in between for Moses because Moses trusted Him
- Even though Moses asked to see God's glory and couldn't (it would have consumed him), God found a way—He put Moses in a cleft of the rock, covered him with His hand, and let him see His character instead

## The Ten Plagues: God Speaking to the Egyptians

The plagues weren't arbitrary punishment. They were designed to show the Egyptians that their gods were worthless and undeserving of worship.

**The Strategy:**
- Frogs everywhere on the day of frog worship—showing the absurdity of praying to frogs
- Flies and lice made the land stink—their gods lay dead at their feet
- The firstborn protected by their gods—completely unprotected

**What This Shows:**
- God loved the Egyptians and wanted to win them to worship the true God
- He used dramatic power to break through their hard hearts and show them their gods were impotent
- Some Egyptians got the message and went out with Israel—though many went for the wrong reason (fear of power instead of love of God)
- Threatening people with destruction doesn't win their trust or love—it only scares them into obedience

## Pharaoh's Hardened Heart

The record says three different things about Pharaoh's heart—all in the same short passage:
- "God hardened his heart"
- "His heart was hardened" (passive)
- "He hardened his own heart"

**What This Shows:**
- All three statements are simultaneously true
- As God presented more evidence of His power and trustworthiness, Pharaoh's resistance only grew stronger
- The very acts meant to convince Pharaoh of God's reality and power pushed him further away
- This shows how the same evidence can harden one person's heart and soften another's, depending on their initial stance toward God
