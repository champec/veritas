# Proverbs: God's Character Revealed

*(Background: Proverbs is a collection of wise sayings about everyday living, gathered largely by Solomon early in his reign—"during his idealistic days," when, by the record, he "had a clear conception of the Creator" and knew God well. It is a book of plain common sense: how to work, speak, handle money, choose companions, raise children, and avoid ruin. It sits in the middle of the Bible, surrounded by theology and history, and the central question of this study is why—what a book of common sense reveals about the God it points to. A poignant background fact frames the whole book: the same Solomon who wrote this wisdom later made a monumental fool of himself, which is the story of Ecclesiastes.)*

---

## God Is Not Arbitrary — His Commands Make Good Sense

This is the heart of what Proverbs reveals about God. A whole book of plain common sense sits in the middle of Scripture because God's character and government are *not arbitrary*: His commands make good sense, and real religion is a sensible, trustworthy way of life—not a list of demands disconnected from reason.

The recording draws this out several ways:

- **Religion is a whole way of life, not just a legal standing.** God "does not desire sacrifice and ceremony"; what He wants is children who "behave in responsible, dignified ways" and are "safe to have around." If Sabbath-keeping, tithing, health reform, and the rest do not make us into decent people who are pleasant to live next door to, then "it's a total waste of time." Genuine religion shows up as ordinary, sensible, reliable living.

- **God will not violate common sense.** His advice makes sense "at home and in the garden and everywhere." Theology that destroys human reason—such as "love me or I'll burn you for eternity"—is a *misrepresentation* of God, not a revelation of Him. Even a child's honest question ("how come the king has nothing on?") should never be brushed aside as something you "just have to believe" and file away in a separate compartment from real life. What is true of God will not contradict the common sense of a thoughtful child.

- **We become like the God we worship.** A person who admires God for "His wise and gracious ways" grows into a disciplined, poised, wise, gracious person. A reasonable God produces reasonable people. (This is also why a God pictured as arbitrary or unreasonable is so damaging—worshipers become like the god they imagine.)

**What this shows about God:**
- God is not arbitrary. His commands and His government make good sense, and the proof of genuine religion is that it produces sensible, trustworthy, decent lives. *(Recorded as Principle 12 in `broader-concepts.md`.)*

## Sin's Harm Is Self-Inflicted, Not an Imposed Penalty (Proverbs 5 & 7)

The long warnings against the seductive adulteress (Proverbs 5 and 7) state plainly how ruin comes: "The iniquities of the wicked ensnare him, and he is caught in the toils of his sin… he dies for lack of discipline." The recording stops to ask directly: "Does that sound like an imposed penalty, or a natural consequence?"—and answers, a consequence of one's own doing. Like a student who fails an exam because he did not prepare (not because God withholds a passing grade to punish him), the harm God warns against is the built-in result of the wrong course, not an arbitrary penalty God adds from outside. This is why God's warnings are loving protection, not threats.

*(This reinforces the interpretation principle "What God Warns Against Is Natural Consequence, Not Arbitrary Punishment" in `biblical-interpretation.md`, and supports Principle 12: a God whose warnings describe real cause-and-effect is not arbitrary.)*

## Freedom Is So Real That Even Perfect Parenting Cannot Guarantee Loyalty (Proverbs 22:6)

"Train up a child in the way he should go, and when he is old he will not depart from it" is a general maxim, not an ironclad guarantee. The recording presses the point with a striking argument: God Himself was the perfect parent, in a perfect environment, with perfectly healthy children—and He still "lost" a third of His angels when Lucifer rebelled. If even God's flawless upbringing could not prevent His children from leaving, then a child who walks away does not prove the parents failed; it proves that freedom is genuinely real. We "train" animals but *educate* people, because people have a free will that can accept or refuse.

**What this shows about God:**
- God gives real freedom even to those closest to Him and raised best by Him; He will not override it, and the possibility of refusal is the price of that freedom. *(Reinforces Principle 5, "God Values Freedom." That proverbs like this are general maxims rather than guarantees is treated in `biblical-interpretation.md`.)*

## God Fully Restores the Returning Failure (Solomon)

The book's frame is itself a revelation. Solomon wasted more than most people will ever have and disgraced himself. Yet when he came back, God did not seat him "on the back row" or bar him from service. Instead God said, in effect, "I'd like you to write another book in the Bible"—numbering the returned prodigal "among the holy men of God." The story of Solomon is the prodigal son told centuries in advance: the failure comes home and is fully taken back.

**What this shows about God:**
- God restores the returning failure completely, treating the disgrace as if it were not there. *(Reinforces Principle 6, "God Is Gracious to His Enemies," which already lists Solomon's full reinstatement.)*

---

## Added Evidence: God Restores the Dignity of the Devalued

Proverbs honors a woman more highly than almost anywhere in ancient literature (the capable woman of Proverbs 31, praised above charm and beauty for her wisdom and strength). The recording extends this to how Jesus treated the women society devalued: He deliberately, repeatedly worked to recover the "dignity and self-respect" of the woman known for an immoral life, and surprised His disciples by openly honoring the Samaritan woman. He risked His own reputation to restore theirs.

This is not a separate trait but added evidence of how God treats His failing children and the people others write off—the same graciousness already seen in His full reinstatement of returning sinners. *(Recorded as an example under Principle 7, "God Is Gentle With His Failing Children.")*

---

## Overall Proverbs Picture

Proverbs reveals a God who is not arbitrary: His commands make good sense, and the test of real religion is whether it turns out sensible, trustworthy, decent people who are safe to live beside. Its warnings describe natural cause-and-effect rather than imposed penalties; its honored woman and Jesus' treatment of the devalued show God restoring dignity to those others write off; its picture of even perfectly raised children remaining free underscores how real God's gift of freedom is; and its very existence—written by the restored prodigal Solomon—shows God fully taking back the returning failure. (How to read "the fear of the Lord," and why proverbs are general maxims rather than proof-texts or guarantees, are treated in `biblical-interpretation.md`.)
