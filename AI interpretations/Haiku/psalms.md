# Psalms: God's Character Revealed

*(Background: Psalms is the hymn book and prayer book of Israel—150 songs and prayers by many authors, mostly David, but also Asaph, the sons of Korah, and even one by Moses. Because they are poetry, the feelings in them are expressed very dramatically ("every night I flood my bed with tears"). They are best understood not as a collection of detachable proof-texts but as whole songs, each carrying one movement of experience. Crucially, they include not only sublime praise but also raw cries of despair, abandonment, and even demands for vengeance on enemies—prayers that are clearly far from God's ideal, yet which God inspired and preserved. The central question of this study is: what do these honest, sometimes shocking prayers reveal about the God to whom they are addressed?)*

---

## God Invites Us to Express Ourselves Honestly and Openly

This is the heart of what Psalms reveals about God. God wants the communication between us and Him to be **real**, not pretended.

The reasoning runs like this. Psalm 139 says God already knows us completely: "You know when I sit down and when I rise up; you discern my thoughts from afar… even before a word is on my tongue, you know it altogether." If God already knows everything inside us, then pretending in His presence is pointless and faintly absurd—like telling a doctor you feel fine while he reads your X-ray. So God invites us to drop the pretense and tell Him the truth, whatever it is.

And the truth in the Psalms is often raw. The psalmists pour out depression ("Will the Lord spurn forever? Has God forgotten to be gracious?"—Ps 77), the feeling of being abandoned ("My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"—Ps 22), and even open hatred and a desire for vengeance ("Do I not hate them that hate you, O Lord? I hate them with perfect hatred"—Ps 139; "Happy shall he be who… dashes your little ones against the rock"—Ps 137). No voice comes down from heaven rebuking the psalmist for praying like that.

The decisive proof is that **God preserved these prayers as models.** The recording's argument: if it were wrong to pray so candidly, the Spirit who inspired Scripture would never have inspired and kept these prayers, because that would mislead every later generation. Instead they were kept—which means God is not offended by honest, even ugly, self-expression. The only thing that could disappoint Him is the opposite: pretending, being artificial, "keeping up a saintly image" on our knees.

We know God honors this kind of honesty because of how He treated **David**. David was a man of blood who killed on a scale that is hard to read about, and who prayed for the ruin of his enemies. God did not endorse any of that. Yet David is called a man after God's own heart—because, having spoken his real feelings, he could also say, "Search me, O God, and know my heart… and see if there be any wicked way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting" (Ps 139). That willingness to be honest and then be searched and changed is the thing God honored.

**What this shows about God:**
- God wants our communication with Him to be genuine and unguarded. He already knows us, so He invites us to express ourselves honestly and openly—and He is not offended by the truth. What disappoints Him is pretense. *(Recorded as Principle 11 in `broader-concepts.md`.)*

**The healthy balance:** honesty is not the end of the matter. The model prayers move from candor ("Lord, you know I hate this man") to surrender ("search me… if I need a new heart and a right spirit, I'd like that"). God welcomes the raw truth so that healing can begin there.

## God's "Vengeance" Aims to Win, Not Destroy

The Psalms repeatedly cry out for vengeance, and Scripture answers, "Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord" (cf. Ps 94; Romans 12:19). The recording draws out *why* God reserves vengeance to Himself, and it is a revelation of His character: God's way of "repaying" an enemy is to discipline him in hopes of winning him back.

The striking implication: if you hand your enemy over to God's vengeance, you may be startled to "meet your enemy in the Kingdom," because God, loving him too, will use even discipline to try to save him. Human vengeance aims to destroy; God's aim is to redeem. That is exactly why it is safer to leave vengeance to Him.

**What this shows about God:**
- Even God's "vengeance" is redemptive—He would rather win an enemy than destroy him. *(Reinforces Principle 6, "God Is Gracious to His Enemies.")*

## God Meets the Fighting Man Where He Is

David's war-prayers and his thanksgiving for victory in battle are a vivid case of God meeting people far from His ideal. God never wanted Israel to fight at all; yet, working with them where they were, He "helped David fight though He never wanted him to," and recorded David's blood-soaked feelings without correcting every point from heaven. The closing prayer of the study states the principle directly: God listens to our cries—even our cries for vengeance—and then leads us, "but no faster than we are able to follow."

The severe language in some psalms fits the same pattern. In Psalm 2, God's voice is fierce—"his wrath is quickly kindled," "you shall break them with a rod of iron." This is the same "sign-language" seen at Sinai: God speaks severely only when settled rebellion has made the gentle voice useless, and only to regain a hearing.

**What this shows about God:**
- God meets people in conditions far below His ideal—even helping a fighting man fight—and leads them forward gradually, no faster than they can follow. *(Reinforces Principle 3, "God Meets Us Where We Are.")*

## God Desires a Willing Ear, Not Ritual

Psalm 40:6–8 sets ritual against relationship: "Sacrifice and offering you did not desire, but you have given me an open ear… burnt offering and sin offering you have not required. Then I said, 'Lo, I come… I delight to do your will, O my God.'" The "open ear" is the willingness to *listen*—which in Scripture means to harken, and so to trust and obey. What God wants is not the performance of sacrifice but a heart willing to hear Him.

**What this shows about God:**
- God desires a willing ear—a readiness to listen and respond—rather than ritual performance. *(Reinforces Principle 7, "God Is Gentle With His Failing Children," and its "willingness to listen, not perfect performance" thread.)*

---

## Overall Psalms Picture

Above everything, Psalms reveals a God who wants reality from us. Because He already knows us completely, He invites us to express ourselves honestly and openly—despair, abandonment, even hatred—without pretense, and He honors that honesty (as He honored David) when it is joined to a willingness to be searched and changed. Alongside this, the Psalms reinforce that God meets people where they are and leads them gradually, that His "vengeance" is aimed at winning rather than destroying, and that He desires a willing ear over ritual.
