# Biblical Interpretation and Understanding

## The Bible Is a Continuous Story

The Bible should be read as one continuous, developing story of God's interaction with people. Because of this, we must distinguish between **what God did in a specific context** and **what God ultimately wants as His ideal**.

God does not change. What changes is the context: the people, their fears, their hardness, their capacity to understand, their culture, and the moment in the larger story. This means God may work within imperfect customs or limited understanding without those things becoming His final will.

The divorce laws are an example: God gave laws regulating divorce, but Jesus later says, "from the beginning it was not so." That means God acted within a temporary human situation while the deeper principle remained unchanged.

Deuteronomy gives several examples of this. The Sabbath commandment is connected with creation in Exodus and deliverance in Deuteronomy, showing that repeated biblical material may carry more than one meaning. Deuteronomy 14 includes instructions that make sense only when read in context, not as a flat code book. These examples show why the whole story must be kept together before forming a broad principle.

For that reason, broad principles should not be built from isolated stories. The whole Bible must be kept in mind. Later passages may clarify earlier ones; earlier stories may explain later ones. The clearest revelation of God is Jesus, so difficult Old Testament stories must be understood in a way that can finally be reconciled with Him.

The Bible itself recognizes this difference. God spoke plainly with Moses, but at other times He used dreams, symbols, ceremonies, rituals, and dark speech. So when a story makes God appear harsh or severe, the question is not whether God changed, but what context required that form of action or communication.

**Working question:**

Is this story showing God's final ideal, or is it showing how God met people in a specific context on the way toward His ideal?

## God's Blessing Does Not Mean God's Endorsement

A recurring interpretive trap is to assume that because God blessed something, He approved of it. The biblical record shows the opposite repeatedly.

- God blessed Solomon with extraordinary wisdom, yet Solomon abused it.
- God helped David fight and win, yet He never wanted Israel fighting at all—that situation came from their refusal to enter Canaan His way.
- God blessed the monarchy and chose its kings, after plainly saying He would rather be their king.
- God blessed David and Bathsheba's later union (Solomon was their second child), though the relationship began in the worst act of David's life.

The lesson is that God's help and blessing in a situation do not signal that the situation is His ideal, or that the conduct involved is endorsed. He works graciously with people where they are. Therefore, a story should never be read as "God blessed it, so it must be right." The presence of blessing is not the presence of approval.

**Working question:**

Is this blessing a sign that God approves of what is happening, or a sign that God is graciously working with people in a situation that is far from His ideal?

## Illustrations and Parables Usually Make One Point

A story, illustration, or parable is normally built to make a single point. Trying to extract meaning from every detail leads to error.

The rich man and Lazarus is a clear example: Jesus told a vivid story, and when He interpreted it, He drew one point from the end—"if they will not listen to Moses and the prophets, neither will they be convinced if someone rises from the dead." He did not endorse every detail of the picture as literal doctrine. Reading too much into the details (communication between the dead, the geography of the afterlife) misuses the story.

The Micaiah account (1 Kings 22) works the same way. The dramatized heavenly scene—God asking "who will entice Ahab?" and a spirit volunteering to be a "lying spirit"—is making one point: the spirit guiding Ahab's 400 prophets was not the Spirit of truth. It is not a literal transcript of how heaven operates, and its details should not be pressed into doctrine.

**Working question:**

What single point is this story making? Am I building doctrine on details that were only there to serve that one point?

## The Bible Often Speaks in the Language and Beliefs of Its Audience

God communicates in terms the audience can grasp, sometimes using their existing beliefs to make a point, without endorsing those beliefs as true.

- Jesus waited until the fourth day to raise Lazarus and pointed out that the body already stank—because many in His audience believed the spirit lingered near the body for three days. He used their belief to remove all doubt that Lazarus was truly dead, not because the belief was correct.
- Stories may be framed around what the hearers assumed, because that is how the point lands with them.

This means a passage's wording sometimes reflects the audience's framework rather than a literal statement of fact. The meaning must be read in light of who was being addressed and what would communicate to them.

**Working question:**

Is this wording stating a literal fact, or is it framed in the audience's own beliefs and language to make the point land with them?

## What God Warns Against Is Natural Consequence, Not Arbitrary Punishment

A key to reading the difficult parts of the Bible is recognizing that the things God forbids, or warns and tries to protect us against, are the very things that *naturally* destroy us. The harm is not an arbitrary penalty God imposes from outside; it is the built-in result of departing from Him.

- **"They became false" (2 Kings 17):** "They went after false gods... and became false." We become like what we worship. This is described as how reality works, not as a punishment God adds on.
- **The exile (2 Chronicles 36:15–16):** God reached out "till there was no remedy," then let them go. The ruin that followed was the consequence of persistent rejection, not God lashing out.
- **The garden warning ("you shall surely die"):** what looked like a threat is vindicated across the story—people are genuinely destroyed by the natural consequence of separation from the source of life.

This carries a character edge: it answers the accusation that God is arbitrary. Read this way, His warnings are loving protection, and the consequences of ignoring them are not evidence of cruelty but of truthfulness.

**Working question:**

Is this harm an arbitrary penalty God is imposing, or the natural consequence God was warning against and trying to prevent?

## Jesus Is the Clearest Revelation—Read the Severe Stories Through Him

When a story makes God appear harsh, the final test is whether the conclusion can be reconciled with Jesus, who is God's clearest self-revelation ("if you have seen me, you have seen the Father").

- When the disciples wanted to call down fire from heaven "as Elijah did," Jesus refused: "I have not come to destroy but to save" (cf. 2 Kings 1). The same God who once allowed fire on the fifties, when He came in person, would not do it.
- This does not mean God changed; it means the severe Old Testament episodes must be understood in a way that is finally consistent with the character Jesus revealed.

**Working question:**

Can my explanation of this severe story be reconciled with the God revealed in Jesus? If not, I have not yet understood it.

## Blessing-and-Curse Passages Are Not a Flat Cause-and-Effect Formula

Passages that promise blessing for obedience and disaster for disobedience (such as 2 Chronicles 7) must not be read as a mechanical formula: good people always prosper, suffering people must have sinned.

This is exactly the oversimplification Job's three friends made—Job was wealthy, so he must be good; then he lost everything, so he must be wicked. God said the three friends were wrong. Up in heaven, God was calling Job a perfect man and permitting his suffering precisely to show his trust.

Trouble can come for many reasons: living on a dangerous planet, a test God permits (as with Job, and even with good men like Isaiah and Elisha, who suffered illness), not only as a consequence of personal sin. The model must be built from all of Scripture, not from the blessing-and-curse passages alone.

**Working question:**

Am I reading this as a rigid formula (suffering proves sin, prosperity proves goodness), or am I allowing the rest of Scripture—especially Job—to correct that oversimplification?

## Meticulous Rule-Keeping Is Not the Same as Knowing God

A revival can produce careful, devoted, blueprint-following religion and still miss the point entirely.

After the exile, the reforms under Ezra and Nehemiah were so thorough that the Jews never returned to idolatry again. They became the most devout, Bible-studying, commandment-keeping people imaginable—careful even to "strain gnats out of their goat's milk." Yet it was the descendants of these very people who rejected and killed Christ. Their meticulous obedience to the blueprint was not matched by actually knowing the kind of person God is.

This is a warning against reading the Bible as a code book of deeds to be done and sins to be shunned. Careful rule-keeping can coexist with a complete misunderstanding of God's character. The goal of Scripture is to reveal what God is like, not merely to produce correct behavior.

**Working question:**

Is this reading leading me to know God better, or only to keep rules more carefully? Correct behavior without knowing God's character is exactly the mistake that ended in rejecting Christ.
