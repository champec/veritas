# Mark: God's Character Revealed

*(This recording covers the Gospel of Mark in two sessions.)*

## The Rapid Movement of a Non-Genealogical Gospel

The Gospel of Mark is widely agreed to be the earliest and shortest of the synoptic gospels. Directed primarily toward a Roman audience, it features no birth story or detailed genealogy. 

- **A Preoccupation with Action:** The text moves with relentless speed, repeatedly utilizing the Greek words for *"immediately"* and *"straightway."*
- **Peter's Hand Behind the Pen:** Early Christian tradition (passed down by Papias and supported by historical and internal evidence) indicates that Mark wrote down the gospel from the preaching of Peter while they were together in Rome.
- **The Modesty of Peter:** Omissions in Mark are telling. It completely avoids stories that would flatter Peter, such as his walking on the water (Matthew 14) or Jesus’ magnificent commendation of his confession (*"On this rock I will build my church,"* Matthew 16). Yet, Mark includes Peter's failures and his painful three-fold denial with unsparing detail.

**What this shows about God:** God values modesty and transparent, unpretentious relationships. He does not seek flashy self-promotion or require His close friends to burnish their reputation. He values the humble, honest heart that is transparent about its weaknesses.

## Sabbath Rules: The Gift of Rest vs. The Burden of Checklist Legalism

The conflict over the Sabbath in Mark 2 and 3 highlights the contrast between the true character of God and the arbitrary system constructed by the religious leaders (codified as the 39 main categories of work in the Mishnah).

- **The Creator's Defense:** When the disciples plucked grain on a Sabbath walk, Jesus responded: *"The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath"* (Mark 2:27). He defended His hungry disciples, identifying Himself as the Lord of the Sabbath.
- **The Accusers' Vigilance:** In the synagogue immediately following, a man with a withered hand was present. The religious leaders watched Jesus closely—not out of hope that a suffering brother would be healed, but looking for an infraction so they could accuse Him of work.
- **Grieved and Angry at Hardness of Heart:** Jesus confronted them with a question: *"Is it lawful on the Sabbath to do good or to do harm, to save life or to kill?"* When they kept silent, Mark notes that Jesus looked around at them with **anger, deeply grieved at their hardness of heart** (Mark 3:5). 
- **The Badge Without the Heart:** The leaders kept the seventh day with absolute devotion, yet when Jesus healed the man with a silent word, they immediately met with their political enemies (the Herodians) to plot Jesus’ murder. 

**What this shows about God:** God's laws are never arbitrary; they are protective gifts designed for human well-being. Keeping the Sabbath as an empty badge or checklist without its meaning is legalism, which turns His gift of rest into a stressful burden. A person can keep the day with legalistic precision while harboring hatred and murder in their heart, completely misrepresenting God's character.

## Meeting Us Where We Are: Gentle Methods for Weak Faith

Jesus' miracles in Mark reveal an individual-focused style where He accommodates Himself to human limitation.

- **The Blind Man of Bethsaida:** In Mark 8:22–26, Jesus takes a blind man out of the village, spits on his eyes, and lays hands on him. When the man says, *"I see men; they look like trees, walking,"* Jesus touches his eyes a second time, restoring his vision completely. 
- **The Practical Detail of Jairus' Daughter:** In Mark 5:41–43, after raising Jairus's daughter from the dead with simple Aramaic words (*"Talitha cumi"*), Jesus immediately orders the overjoyed, stunned family to *"give her something to eat."*
- **Aids to Faith:** The use of spit or touch was not because saliva had magical healing power. Rather, God stoops to use concrete, tangible steps (resembling ancient therapeutic remedies) to support and encourage a weak person's faith. 

**What this shows about God:** God is an incredibly gentle teacher. He doesn't hold Himself aloof from our weakness or demand instant, perfect belief; He meets us where we are and leads us progressively. He is highly sensitive to simple, practical human needs (like seeing that a newly resurrected child is hungry), showing that His glory lies in His tender compassion for the individual rather than flashy displays of administrative power.

## One Face of Love: Peter and Judas Under the Gaze of Grace

Both Peter and Judas failed Jesus dramatically, and both looked at the exact same face of Christ in their moments of crisis.

*   **Peter's Gaze:** At Peter's third denial, when the rooster crowed twice, Peter looked up to see if Jesus had noticed. Expecting to see wrath, anger, or offended justice from a master he had publicly disowned, Peter instead met a look of deep sorrow, disappointment, and pity. This was the same face of the One who, just hours before, had knelt on His knees and washed Peter's dirty feet. This sorrowful love broke Peter's heart, causing him to flee the courtyard and weep in Gethsemane, eventually leading to his restoration.
*   **Judas's Gaze:** Judas, too, looked at that same face of unretaliatory love, but the depth of that grace was so unbearable to his defensive, self-justifying heart that it drove him to despair and suicide. Jesus had protected Judas's reputation so thoroughly in the Upper Room that the other disciples did not even suspect him. 

**What this shows about God:** God does not look at sinners differently than He looks at saints. His face is constant, unconditional, forgiving love. The same manifestation of His character can cause one person to weep with repentance and another to flee in terror or despair. Under this view, "end-time fire" is not God changing His character to execute His enemies; it is the natural, devastating reaction of closed hearts to the presence of unveiled, infinite love.
