# Joshua & Judges: God's Character Revealed

*(This recording covers Joshua and Judges together, ending with a brief glimpse of Ruth. The detailed stories below are treated as one Joshua–Judges unit.)*

## God Is Gentle With His Failing Children

The book repeatedly shows God putting the best interpretation on imperfect people and asking very little of them.

- **Rahab** was a Canaanite prostitute who knew almost nothing about God. What little she knew was secondhand and mixed with fear—she had only *heard* that He dried up the Red Sea and defeated the kings beyond the Jordan (Joshua 2:8–11). Yet that little was enough; she is named among the heroes of faith (Hebrews 11:31).
- The whole faith-list in Hebrews 11 works this way: **Sarah** laughed and then denied it, **Rahab** lied, **Gideon** needed signs, **Samson** was self-indulgent. Scripture tells the truth about each of them, yet credits them with faith rather than cataloguing their failures.

**What this shows about God:**
- God puts the best possible interpretation on His imperfect children.
- He forgives weakness and asks little, accepting small and flawed faith.

---

## God Is Gracious to His Enemies

God's posture toward those who are estranged from Him, or who fail Him, is consistently gracious.

- **When they come back, He restores them fully.** Samson wasted his gifts and lived self-indulgently, yet when he turned back at the end of his life he is listed among the heroes of faith (Hebrews 11:32). Solomon squandered God-given wisdom, yet on returning was not held at arm's length but fully reinstated. The past is treated as if it were not there.
- **When their faith is weak, He provides help rather than rebuke.** Gideon asked for a wet fleece and then a dry one (Judges 6). God supplied the signs without scolding him.

**What this shows about God:**
- God restores returning people fully and does not hold the past against them.
- He keeps working with people instead of abandoning them.

---

## God Values Freedom

God gives genuinely and leaves people free to use—or misuse—what He gives.

- **Samson** was given extraordinary strength and was free to indulge himself and squander it.
- **Solomon** was given great wisdom and was free to turn it to selfish ends.

God did not revoke or restrain these gifts to force good use of them. The very fact that the gifts *could* be misused shows how real the freedom was.

**What this shows about God:**
- When God gives something, He gives it genuinely and does not control how it is used.
- Freedom matters so much to God that He will not violate it, even with His own gifts.

---

## Overall Joshua–Judges Picture

By human measures, this period looks like failure—little evangelism, gross behavior, few clearly good people (Ruth stands out as a relief). But what comes through about God is consistent: He is gentle with failing people, gracious toward enemies and returners, and unwilling to override the freedom He has given. The difficult and violent material in these books (the stoning of Achan, the Levite's concubine) reflects the debased condition of the people God was working with and is treated separately in `proper-context.md`.
