# Numbers: God's Character Revealed

## God's Anger Is Not Human Temper

Numbers contains several references to God's anger. This raises an important question: does God lose His temper the way humans do?

The answer must be read in light of the larger biblical picture. When Jesus cleansed the temple, guilty people ran, but children and the sick were not afraid of Him. This shows that divine anger is not rage, loss of control, or emotional instability.

**What this shows:**
- God's anger is not like human anger.
- God does not lose control or become unsafe.
- His anger is connected with grief over harm, injustice, rebellion, and misrepresentation.
- The humble and needy do not need to be afraid of Him.

---

## Moses at the Rock: Misrepresenting God's Grace

The people grumbled again because they had no water. God told Moses to speak to the rock and give them water.

Moses instead rebuked the people: "Hear now, you rebels," and struck the rock. This made God look angry with the people when God was actually trying to give them water generously.

The issue was not merely that Moses broke a symbol or disobeyed an instruction. The text says Moses did not trust God enough to show Him as holy before the people. Moses misrepresented God.

**What this shows:**
- God wanted to be seen as gracious toward undeserving people.
- God wanted kindness, not public rebuke, to lead the people toward repentance.
- Misrepresenting God is especially serious when done by someone trusted and influential.
- God values trust and truthful representation.

---

## God's Discipline of Moses Was Not Rejection

Moses was not allowed to enter Canaan. On the surface, this can seem severe: after decades of faithfulness, Moses is excluded because of one failure.

But the larger story shows God did not reject Moses. God showed him the land, buried him, resurrected him, and later honored him by sending him to encourage Jesus on the Mount of Transfiguration.

**What this shows:**
- God takes breaches of trust seriously.
- God disciplines His friends without ceasing to love them.
- God is not unforgiving; He continued to honor Moses.
- Divine discipline and divine rejection are not the same thing.

---

## Balaam: God Lets People Go Their Way but Keeps Warning Them

Balaam asked permission to curse Israel. God said no. Balaam kept asking. Eventually God let him go, but He did not abandon him.

The angel in the road, the donkey that saw what Balaam did not, and the eventual turning of curse into blessing all show God still working to stop Balaam from destroying himself.

**What this shows:**
- God respects freedom, even when people insist on a dangerous path.
- God does not simply abandon people the moment He lets them have their way.
- God places warnings and obstacles in the path of destruction.
- God makes it difficult to be lost.

---

## God Works with Imperfect Methods

Numbers discusses methods and customs that were not God's ideal: casting lots, fighting, food choices, monarchy, divorce customs, polygamy, and cities of refuge. God often worked with people through imperfect customs rather than instantly abolishing them.

This does not mean the customs were ideal. It means God met people where they were and led them no faster than they could follow.

**What this shows:**
- God is patient and practical.
- God does not wait for perfect methods before helping people.
- God can bless imperfect methods without making them His ideal.
- God leads people gradually toward something better.

---

## Overall Numbers Picture

Numbers shows God dealing with grumbling, fear, rebellion, misrepresentation, and stubborn self-will. Yet the strongest picture is not of a God eager to punish. It is of a God who keeps working with difficult people, protects freedom, warns those going wrong, and takes trust so seriously that He will not let even His best friends misrepresent Him without consequence.
