# Lamentations — God's Character

## Overview

Written by Jeremiah during and after the final destruction of Jerusalem in 586 BC, Lamentations is one of the most carefully constructed pieces of writing in the Bible — an elaborate acrostic poem in five sections, each unit beginning with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet. The third section triples each letter (three verses per letter), giving 66 units in that chapter alone. The teacher notes that Jeremiah composed this under the most extreme circumstances imaginable, and that the deliberate literary structure was likely an aid to memory and transmission in an age without printing or copies.

---

## 1. The Book Must Be Read as a Whole

The first half of Lamentations is full of language that sounds like God actively inflicting destruction: *"the LORD has made her suffer," "the LORD inflicted on the day of his fierce anger," "the LORD has become like an enemy."* Taken alone, these verses make God look like the fearsome deity Satan accused Him of being.

But the second half pivots entirely:

*"The steadfast love of the LORD never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning; great is your faithfulness."* (Lamentations 3:22–23)

Both halves must be read together — the same principle as reading the Psalms as complete songs of experience rather than plucking individual verses. The apparent severity of the first half must be understood in light of the steadfast love declared in the second half, and both must be read in light of Jeremiah's own longer testimony: that the people brought this on themselves, and that what appears to be God doing it is God permitting and taking responsibility for what He allows.

---

## 2. God Does Not Willingly Afflict

The turning point of the entire book — sitting at the exact centre — is Lamentations 3:33:

*"He does not willingly afflict or grieve the sons of men."*

Even where discipline falls, God does not want to do it. He does not enjoy it. He does it only while there is still hope. This is Jeremiah's own summary, written in the middle of the worst destruction he ever witnessed, of what he understood about God's heart. The structure of the book places this statement deliberately at the pivot between the catalogue of suffering and the return to trust and hope.

---

## 3. The Book Ends on a Deliberate Question

Lamentations closes without a resolution:

*"Why do you forget us forever? Why do you forsake us so long? Restore us to yourself, O LORD, that we may be restored... Or have you utterly rejected us? Are you exceedingly angry with us?"*

The teacher notes that Jeremiah already knew the answer — he had already recorded God's promise that they would return in seventy years. He ends on the open question as a literary device, to provoke readers to think through what they know about God rather than hand them a conclusion. The very act of ending this way is itself a statement of trust: Jeremiah is confident enough in who God is to leave the question open and let the reader answer it from everything they have already learned.
