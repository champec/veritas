# Matthew: God's Character Revealed

*(This recording covers the intertestamental period and the Gospel of Matthew in two sessions.)*

## The Problem of Expectations: Why Jesus Was Misunderstood

The intertestamental period (from Malachi to Matthew) was a time of mounting national frustration, deep religious zeal, and foreign oppression under the Persians, Greeks, and Romans. 

- **The Expectation of Force:** Under the brutal tyrannical rule of Antiochus Epiphanes, a major Jewish revolt led by Judas Maccabeus cleansed the temple sanctuary. Because Maccabeus achieved military victory, the Jewish nation came to expect a Messiah who would act with a similarly stunning display of outward force and power.
- **The Desire for Coercion:** This expectation is reflected in Jewish literature of the period (such as the Apocrypha and Pseudepigrapha). The people anticipated a Messiah who would reveal His supremacy with *"one flash of overmastering will"* to force an acknowledgment of His rule (Desire of Ages, p. 700). 
- **The Temptation to Manifest Force:** Because the people wanted a display of overpowering divinity, Jesus faced a constant, heavy temptation to compel His enemies by a word or a look. Yet He resolutely refused to use force, holding firmly to His chosen position as one with humanity. This choice made Him highly disappointing and deeply misunderstood by His contemporaries.

**What this shows about God:** God refuses to win the great controversy through coercion or raw power. Even when the entire world expects and demands a display of sovereign force, God wins through the still small voice of truth spoken in love, preserving human freedom above all else.

## Sabbath Rules: Hard-Hearted Legalism vs. Gracious Rest

Following the Babylonian exile, a deep desire to never fail God again led Jewish theologians to construct a meticulous safeguard around the Torah. Over centuries, they codified these rules in the Mishnah (such as the 39 main categories of Sabbath work).

- **The Crushing Blueprint:** This legalistic system became incredibly detailed, regulating whether a person could carry a wallet in their hand (culpable) vs. on the back of their hand (not culpable), what kind of knots could be tied with one hand, or whether an aching tooth could be treated on the Sabbath using vinegar. 
- **The Threat of Jesus:** When Jesus came and healed on the Sabbath, He directly violated these traditional rules. To the religious leaders, this made Jesus appear unbiblical and lawless.
- **The Light Burden:** Jesus confronted this legalism by declaring, *"My yoke is easy, and my burden is light"* (Matthew 11:30). He explained that He was not introducing new laws or abolishing the old; He was simply clearing away the heavy human traditions that misrepresented His Father.

**What this shows about God:** God is not a cosmic inspector waiting to catch His children on technical infractions of rules. He is a loving parental figure whose instructions are sensible, rest-giving, and designed for human well-being, not to create anxiety or fear.

## The Beatitudes: Upside-Down Calculations of Blessing

The prevailing theology of Jesus' day taught that earthly wealth and status were direct indicators of divine approval, while poverty and suffering were signs of God's curse (an idea pushed by Job's three friends).

- **"Happy are the Poor":** Jesus stunned His audience by declaring, *"Blessed are the poor in spirit..."* (Matthew 5:3) / *"Blessed are you poor"* (Luke 6:20). He showed that those who are spiritually self-satisfied (the Laodiceans) cannot be helped, whereas those who recognize their spiritual poverty are ready to receive His healing grace.
- **The Camel and the Needle:** When Jesus noted that *"it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom of God"* (Matthew 19:24), the disciples were astounded, asking, *"Who then can be saved?"* Under their theological system, if the wealthy and outwardly blessed couldn't get in, there was no hope for anyone else.
- **The Heart of the Law:** Jesus' first sermon (the Sermon on the Mount) was regarded as heresy because it challenged this surface-level theology. He pointed out that outward rule-keeping means nothing if the heart is not right: the Tenth Commandment already forbade internal coveting and evil desires; therefore, lust in the heart is already adultery, and hatred is already murder.

**What this shows about God:** God does not judge by human standards of prestige, inheritance, or material wealth. He values the humble, honest heart that recognizes its need for healing. His law is not a checklist of outward behaviors but a transcript of His character written on the heart.

## Hardness of Heart: The Principle of Divine Accommodation

In Matthew 19, the Pharisees attempt to trap Jesus by asking about the law of divorce in Deuteronomy 24. 

- **Original Design vs. Temporary Concession:** Jesus goes back to creation (*"From the beginning it was not so"*), explaining that God's ideal is permanent, loving partnership. He explicitly states that Moses allowed divorce only because of their *"hardness of heart"* (Matthew 19:8).
- **The Hardness of Their Necks:** This highlights a massive Old Testament principle: when God's people demand practices that are far from His ideal (such as divorce, eating the meat of Egypt, demanding an earthly king, or fighting in battles), He does not abandon them. Instead, He stoops to meet them, regulating their imperfect choices to prevent worse evil.

**What this shows about God:** God is incredibly patient and cooperative. He does not demand that we achieve absolute moral perfection before He is willing to walk with us. He meets us in our current condition, gracefully accommodating our limitations while keeping His ultimate ideal in view. His concessions must never be mistaken for His final endorsements.

## Protecting the Dignity of Sinners

Two remarkable stories in the closing chapters of Matthew demonstrate how Jesus interacted with those who failed.

- **Simon's Feast and Mary:** When Mary anointed Jesus' feet with costly ointment (Matthew 26/Luke 7), Simon the Pharisee (who was healed of leprosy) thought Jesus could not be a prophet because He let a *"sinful woman"* touch Him. In his heart, Simon believed a holy God would keep such people at a distance. Jesus knew Simon's thoughts and told a gentle story of two debtors.
- **Spared from Humiliation:** Because Simon had secretly led his niece (Mary) into her life of sin, a public exposure would have ruined his social standing. Yet Jesus spoke privately to Simon's conscience, writing the sins of his accusers in the dust and sparing Simon from public disgrace. This extraordinary grace completely won Simon's heart, turning him into a devoted follower.
- **Judas's Cover:** The night before He died, Jesus knew Judas was about to betray Him. Yet He protected Judas's reputation so thoroughly at the table that when Jesus told him, *"What you are going to do, do quickly,"* the other disciples assumed Judas was merely going to give to the poor. 

**What this shows about God:** God knows our worst records, yet He takes no pleasure in embarrassing or exposing us. He actively guards the dignity of His failing children, using the absolute minimum force necessary to reach our hearts. This demonstrates that we have nothing to fear from the infinite memory of God in the hereafter, because He will always treat His children with dignity and respect.

## Notes on What Is NOT Offended or Speculated

- **Prophecy as Parallels:** Matthew quotes Old Testament predictions frequently (such as the virgin birth in Isaiah 7, the flight to Egypt in Hosea 11, and Rachel weeping). These are treated not as single-line, crystal-ball predictions, but as beautiful, divinely inspired parallels where Matthew highlights how Christ's life echoes the continuous pattern of God's dealings with Israel.
- **Purity of Canonical Gospels:** The bizarre, aggressive, or silly miracles of the childhood of Jesus found in the New Testament Apocrypha (such as cursing playmates to death, or making clay birds fly) are shown to be clearly counterfeit. Their presence in early centuries highlights by contrast how distinct, balanced, and character-focused the 66 canonical books are.
