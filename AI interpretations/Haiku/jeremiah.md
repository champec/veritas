# Jeremiah — God's Character

## Overview

Jeremiah prophesied for roughly forty years across the three invasions of Nebuchadnezzar, from the reign of Josiah through the final destruction of Jerusalem. He saw the northern ten tribes already gone into Assyrian oblivion and watched the two southern tribes slide toward Babylon. The teacher makes the observation that as the background gets darker, the picture of God becomes clearer — as if God tries ever more desperately to communicate with a people who will not listen.

## 1. God Never Wanted Religion — He Wanted Friendship

One of the most important passages in the book (Jeremiah 7:21–23):

*"In the day that I brought your fathers out of Egypt, I did not speak to them or command them concerning burnt offerings and sacrifices. But this command I gave them: Obey my voice, and I will be your God, and you shall be my people."*

The entire ceremonial system — the tabernacle, the priests, the sacrifices, the Ten Commandments, the Ark of the Covenant — was never God's original desire. He gave these emergency measures to people coming out of four centuries of slavery, people who had been told what to do, how to do it, and almost how to think. They were "too hard of hearing" for the kind of direct friendship God preferred. The visible, repeatable forms were accommodations — God meeting a very immature people where they actually were.

Jeremiah 3:15–16 goes further: God says a day will come when there are good shepherds, the law is written on hearts, and the Ark "will never come to mind, nor shall it be remembered... for it was a temporary emergency measure."

The new covenant (Jeremiah 31:31–34) is the return to the original vision: God's law written on hearts, everyone knowing Him directly, no one needing to tell their neighbour "know the Lord" — because all will know Him. This is exactly what He said coming out of Egypt: "I will be their God, and they shall be my people." The emergency is over; the original desire is restored.

## 2. What God Most Wants to Be Known For

Jeremiah 9:23–24:

*"Let not the wise man glory in his wisdom, let not the mighty man glory in his might, let not the rich man glory in his riches; but let him who glories glory in this: that he understands and knows me — that I am the LORD who practices kindness, justice, and righteousness in the earth, for in these things I delight."*

God does not want to be known for power, sovereignty, or religious authority. He wants to be known as a person who can be personally understood — and who delights in kindness, justice, and righteousness. The teacher notes that Jeremiah was very proud of this knowledge of God, and that it is the single most important thing a person can boast about.

## 3. Sin Chastises Itself — God Is Not the One Inflicting It

Jeremiah 2:17–19:

*"Have you not brought this upon yourself by forsaking the LORD your God?... Your wickedness will chastise you, and your apostasy will reprove you."*

And in 7:19:

*"Is it really I whom they provoke? Is it not themselves, to their own confusion?"*

This is one of the clearest pre-New Testament statements that what looks like divine punishment is actually people experiencing the built-in consequences of their own choices. God is not inflicting. They are reaping. Paul later makes the same point explicitly in Romans 1. It did not originate with Paul; it is already here in Jeremiah.

---

## 4. God Persisted Day After Day

Jeremiah 7:25:

*"From the day that your fathers came out of the land of Egypt to this day, I have persistently sent all my servants the prophets to them, day after day."*

The word "persistently," day after day, across more than eight hundred years — this is God himself describing the length and frequency of His reaching. This is also the God who, in Jeremiah 31:20, describes the northern tribes He has had to release as "my dear son... my darling child," whose heart "yearns" for them even as He lets them go.

---

## 5. God's Heart Yearns Even for Those He Lets Go

Jeremiah 31:20:

*"Is Ephraim my dear son? Is he my darling child? For as often as I speak against him, I do remember him still. Therefore my heart yearns for him; I will surely have mercy on him, says the LORD."*

The ten northern tribes have already been taken into Assyrian captivity. God has had to speak harshly against them repeatedly. But even having released them, He calls them His darling child and says His heart yearns. The letting go described in broader themes across the Bible is here given its most personal and tender expression. God does not release people coldly; He grieves.

## Connections to Broader Concepts

- Jeremiah 7:25 (day after day, persistent sending) adds to Principle 9 (God Goes to Extreme Lengths Before Letting Anyone Go)
- Jeremiah 2:17–19 and 7:19 (wickedness will chastise you; is it not themselves) adds to Principle 12 (God Is Not Arbitrary — warnings are cause-and-effect)
- Jeremiah 31:20 (my heart yearns for Ephraim) adds to Principle 8 (God Ultimately Lets Go Those Who Reject His Love — with grief, not coldness)
