# Joel & Amos: God's Character Revealed

*(This recording covers Joel and Amos in two sessions.)*

## The "Day of the Lord" Is a Time of Final Decision — Personal and Collective

Joel and Amos develop the "day of the Lord" as a recurring theme with multiple layers.

- It is always described as **near** — not because God is deceiving, but because the end is near for every individual at the moment of decision or death.
- It can be **personal**: when a person makes their final commitment, or when they perish and next face God — that is their day of the Lord.
- It can be **collective**: the ten tribes experienced their day of the Lord with Assyria; Judah with Babylon.
- The **ultimate** day of the Lord is the final settling of all questions at the end of the millennium, when the whole universe watches the last events of the great controversy and the earth is made new.
- Amos 5:18 warns: "Woe to you who desire the day of the Lord! Why would you want that day? It is darkness, not light." Some look forward to it expecting reward, only to find it is darkness for them.

**What this shows about God:** God does not want the day of the Lord to catch anyone by surprise. He describes it as near so that each person settles their peace with Him beforehand. Those who are His friends can look forward to it; those who are not will find it terrifying.

## God Delays the Natural Consequences of Sin Through Intervention

The constant "nearness" of the end, followed by delay, is not a threat — "repent or I'll destroy you." It is God actively holding back the natural results of sin so people have time.

- 2 Peter 3:9 — "The Lord is not slow about His promise as some count slowness, but is long-suffering toward you, **not wishing that any should perish but that all should reach repentance**."
- This is the same pattern from Eden: Adam and Eve were warned "you shall surely die," yet they did not die immediately — God veiled His glory, intervened, and gave them time. He has been doing this ever since.
- God goes to great lengths to prevent people from reaping the full consequences of their choices. He delays, intervenes, shields, and holds back — not to threaten, but to give every person room to turn around.
- Jonah: God said "40 days" but relented when Nineveh repented. God was pleased to put off the day of destruction. Jonah didn't like it; God did.
- The kindness of God leads to repentance (Romans 2:4) — and His kindness is particularly shown in His waiting and waiting and waiting.
- God bears the burden of foreknowledge: knowing how people will turn out, yet giving them every chance anyway.

**What this shows about God:** God does not stand by passively while sin destroys people. He actively intervenes to delay the natural consequences, veiling His glory, holding back ruin, and giving every person time to turn. His waiting is not a threat — it is the kindness that gives room for repentance.

## God Rejects Worship That Is Not Matched by Character

This is the central message of Amos — and it connects to Hosea's "I never knew you."

- Israel kept the Sabbath, observed the new moon, offered sacrifices, paid tithe, came early to song service, and sang vigorously. Yet God said: "I hate and despise your festivals... let me have no more of your noisy hymns. My ears are closed to the music of your harps." (Amos 5:21–23)
- They watched the sun go down and cried, "When will the Sabbath be over, that we may sell grain?" — while cheating with biased scales, trampling the needy, and selling the poor for silver (Amos 8:4–6).
- "Pretentious piety is nauseating to the Lord." The most religious people can be the least saveable if their hearts are not in it.
- What God wanted instead: "Let justice roll on like a mighty river, and integrity flow like a never-failing stream." (Amos 5:24)

**What this shows about God:** God is not impressed by worship performance. What He wants is integrity, justice, and honesty — worship that matches the way we treat people during the week. Sabbath-keeping without character is a fraud.

## To Know God Is to Love God (Reinforced)

Joel and Amos reinforce Hosea's key insight.

- Joel 2:27 — "Then you shall know that I am in the Lord your God, and there is no other." Knowing God follows restoration — and results in proclamation (Joel 2:28, quoted at Pentecost).
- Amos ends with the contrast: those who "know" God technically (like Lucifer) but don't know Him in the biblical sense of love, trust, and admiration.
- "The only have I known" / "Go away, I never knew you" — the biblical word "know" implies friendship, intimacy, trust.
- Only the one who is **willing to do His will** will know and understand (John 7:17). Technical knowledge without willingness does not produce understanding.

**What this shows about God:** God is not looking for people who know *about* Him. He is looking for friends who know Him in trust and love — and that knowing always shows itself in a willingness to listen and do His will.

## God's People Can Misread the Signs — Even While Keeping the Blueprint

The Jews looked forward to the Messiah's coming — but for the wrong reasons.

- They expected a warrior king who would lead them against their enemies and establish Jerusalem as the capital of the world.
- When Jesus said "Blessed are the meek," they were alienated — you can't beat the Romans with meekness.
- They had the right day, the right tithe, the right Bible, the right descent from Abraham — and they misunderstood the Messiah completely.
- The warning: "If they could be wrong, could we be wrong?" Keeping the blueprint does not guarantee we understand God's character.

**What this shows about God:** God is not honored by correct performance combined with a wrong picture of who He is. The Jews' mistake is a warning to every generation that keeps the rules but misses the Person.

## The Holy Spirit's Work Produces Self-Control, Not Frenzy

Joel's promise of the Spirit (Joel 2:28) is tested by its fruit.

- The fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22) includes love, joy, peace — and the last one, "temperance," actually means **self-discipline, self-mastery, self-control** (not merely abstinence from alcohol/tobacco).
- Where the Spirit is genuinely working, there is calm self-control, law and order, and decency. People stop behaving like little children (1 Corinthians).
- Where meetings unravel into frenzy — "just let go, stop thinking, clap your hands" — that is not the Spirit's work.
- The ideal: a group of people with love and mutual respect, eager to learn the truth and follow the evidence, patient with each other even when they disagree.

**What this shows about God:** God's Spirit produces self-control, truth-seeking, and mutual respect — not emotional frenzy. Where these are absent, the Spirit is not the author, however much "love" is talked about.

## God's Reputation Suffers When His People Misbehave

Joel 2:17 — "Spare your people, O Lord... why should the nations say, 'Where is their God?'"

- Because God had to withdraw blessings from His people, the surrounding nations concluded Israel's God was weak, tired, or asleep like Baal.
- God's name was blasphemed among the Gentiles because of the behavior of His chosen people (Romans 2:24).
- The positive version: "Seven people will take hold of the robe of a Jew and say, 'Let us go with you, because it is obvious that God is with you.'" (Zechariah 8:23)

**What this shows about God:** God's reputation in the world is tied to the behavior of the people who bear His name. When His people live with integrity, the world is drawn to Him; when they don't, He is misrepresented.

## God Can Use Non-Professional Prophets

Amos 7:14 — "I am neither a prophet nor the son of a prophet; I am a shepherd and a tender of sycamore trees."

- Amos was not a professional prophet trained in the schools. God called him from his flocks and sent him to prophesy.
- The point: God's authority rests on the message, not the office. A shepherd delivering God's word speaks with the same authority as a career prophet.

**What this shows about God:** God is not dependent on credentials or professional training. He calls whom He wills, and His authority rests on the truth of the message, not the status of the messenger.

## God's Warnings Are Invitations to Repent — He Relents When People Turn

Joel 2:13–14 — "Return to the Lord your God, for He is gracious and merciful, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love, and relents from sending calamity."

- God's stated warnings are not threats from a powerful figure who will destroy those who cross Him. They are invitations to turn around.
- He told Nineveh "40 days" — but relented when they repented. God was pleased to put off the day of destruction.
- This is not a gracious mob boss sparing those who double-cross Him. It is God giving chances — the same character He has shown from Eden onward, intervening to delay natural consequences so people have room to return.
- When God "relents," it reflects His consistent character of mercy, not inconsistency.

**What this shows about God:** God's warnings are given to invite repentance, not to lock in destruction. He is always ready to relent when people turn — not because He changes His mind, but because mercy is His consistent character.

## The Famine of the Word Is Not Lack of Bibles

Amos 8:11–12 — "I will send a famine on the land, not of bread or water, but of hearing the words of the Lord. They shall run to and fro, seeking the word of the Lord, but shall not find it."

- This is not about Bibles being destroyed. The Bible has never been more available.
- It is possible to possess the Bible and still have a famine — because you don't understand what it means (like the eunuch reading Isaiah before Philip explained it).
- Even technical knowledge of Biblical matters does not guarantee understanding. Professors can know vast amounts about the Bible and not be believers — "only the one who is willing to do His will will know and understand" (John 7:17).
- The famine is ultimately about people looking for a God of their own imagination, not the God who really is.

**What this shows about God:** God's word is not just information — it requires a willingness to do His will to be understood. Where willingness is absent, the most available Bible produces no understanding.

---

## God Ends With Restoration — Even After Severe Warnings

Like many prophets, Amos ends with a picture of restoration (Amos 9:11–15).

- "In that day I will raise up the booth of David... I will rebuild it as in the days of old."
- "The plowman shall overtake the reaper, and the treader of grapes him who sows seed."
- "I will plant them in their own land, and never again shall they be uprooted."
- Even the most severely critical books end with promises of good things to look forward to.

**What this shows about God:** God's final word is always restoration. Even after the most severe warnings and discipline, His purpose is to rebuild, restore, and make things new. He does not leave His people in ruin.

## Notes on What Is NOT Claimed

- The locusts of Joel (literal vs. symbolic of invading armies) are left open — "does it really make any difference? I don't think it does."
- The "latter rain" is treated cautiously — it may be falling without anyone knowing, and it is not measured by miracles or baptism counts but by the fruit of the Spirit.
- The dating of Joel (9th century to post-exilic) is acknowledged as uncertain.
- The "king of the North" / Armageddon discussion is deferred to Revelation.
