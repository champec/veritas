# The Proper Context

## Purpose

This file tracks difficult Bible stories that, on the surface, can make God appear severe, harsh, arbitrary, vengeful, or exactly like the kind of person Satan accused Him of being.

The goal is not to excuse harshness with weak answers. The goal is to understand the full context of the story well enough to see what it reveals about God's character.

A valid explanation must show how God's action is consistent with love, trustworthiness, freedom, and His desire to save—not merely that He had enough authority to act.

---

## What Does Not Count as an Explanation

These statements are not sufficient explanations by themselves:

- **"God gave them many chances."** Giving chances does not, by itself, explain a severe action.
- **"God was long-suffering before punishing."** Delay before punishment does not, by itself, justify the punishment.
- **"God needed to punish sin."** This is too general and does not explain why this specific action was loving, necessary, or consistent with God's character.
- **"They deserved it."** Deserving punishment does not explain what the action reveals about God.
- **"God is sovereign, so He can do it."** Authority is not character explanation.

---

## Required Standard

For each difficult story, the explanation must answer:

**What does this story show about the kind of person God is when understood in its proper context?**

A strong entry should include:

1. **The surface problem** — why the story appears harsh or severe.
2. **The broader context** — what larger conflict, need, danger, misunderstanding, or circumstance is present.
3. **What God is trying to accomplish** — not merely what He stops or punishes.
4. **How love is acting** — how the action fits God's trustworthiness, respect for freedom, and desire to save.
5. **What false picture is corrected** — how the proper context prevents the story from supporting Satan's accusation against God.

---

## Method for Finding the Proper Context

Difficult stories should not be explained in isolation. The solution usually comes by reading around the text and locating the story within the larger biblical pattern.

When studying a difficult story:

1. **Read before and after the story**  
	Look for the immediate setting, what problem has developed, what God is responding to, and what happens afterward.

2. **Read further in Scripture**  
	Later biblical writers may explain, reinterpret, or place the story in a larger frame.

3. **Read backward in Scripture**  
	Earlier stories may show the pattern God is continuing or adapting.

4. **Compare similar stories**  
	Gather multiple examples of similar divine actions before forming a principle. One isolated story is not enough.

5. **Use broader concepts for light**  
	Existing character principles may help explain why God acted as He did, but they must not be forced onto the text.

6. **Look for the specific goal of the intervention**  
	Severe actions appear limited and directed, not random or constant. Ask what God was preserving, revealing, preventing, or making possible in that moment.

7. **Form a principle only when grounded**  
	A general principle should come from multiple examples and clear contextual evidence, not from a single difficult story.

---

## Important Distinction: Temporary Intervention vs. Final Consequence

Do not assume every severe action works the same way as the final consequence of sin.

The final consequence for those who reject love appears to be God letting them go. This is seen in themes such as:

- God grieving over giving people up: “How can I give you up?”
- God giving people up when they do not want Him.
- The cross, where Jesus was given up as sinners finally are.
- **The exile (2 Chronicles 36:15–16):** God reached out persistently "till there was no remedy," and only then let His people go. This explicitly confirms that the final consequence is God releasing those who will not listen—not arbitrary punishment, but the point at which there is nothing more love can do.

Because of that, explanations of difficult stories should be careful. If God intervenes dramatically before the end, the question is not simply “Why did God punish?” The better question is:

**Why did God intervene here, in this specific way, at this specific time, when He does not always act this way?**

This suggests that many severe interventions may be temporary, limited, and directed toward a specific purpose—such as preserving the possibility of good, maintaining contact with humanity, correcting a destructive misunderstanding, or revealing something necessary in the larger conflict.

But each case must be grounded in its own context. Do not use this as a blanket explanation.

## Stories To Explore

### Genesis

- **The prohibition and expulsion from Eden** — Why were Adam and Eve expelled after their first offense? Why not simply give them a second chance inside the garden?

- **Sodom and Gomorrah** — Why destroy the cities with fire? What is the broader context beyond “they were wicked”?
- **Lot's wife** — Why such a severe consequence for looking back?

### Exodus

- **The Ten Plagues** — Why use such destructive signs, especially the death of the firstborn? How does the context of Egypt's gods and power-based religion affect the meaning?
- **Mount Sinai** — Why reveal Himself with thunder, smoke, earthquake, and fear if perfect love casts out fear?

### Leviticus

- **Nadab and Abihu** — Why did fire come out from God and consume them? Why was their action treated so seriously?
- **The sacrificial system** — Why would God command animal sacrifice if He does not need appeasement or enjoy death?

### Numbers

- **Moses striking the rock** — Why was Moses kept from entering Canaan after one failure, when the people had done worse things repeatedly?
- **God's anger in Numbers** — What does it mean when God is described as angry? How is divine anger different from human loss of temper?
- **Balaam** — Why did God finally let Balaam go after first saying no? What does the donkey and angel episode show about God still warning someone who insists on his own way?
- **The fiery serpents** — Why did God allow or send serpents among the people, and why was the bronze serpent the chosen remedy?
- **The quail/manna story** — Why did the people's demand for different food end so severely?

### 1 & 2 Samuel

- **Uzzah struck dead for touching the Ark (2 Samuel 6)** — Why such an immediate consequence for steadying the Ark? Compare with other "irreverence" stories (the she-bears; Nadab and Abihu) before forming a conclusion. The 2 Samuel recording groups these together but does not resolve them; hold until the pattern can be studied together.
- **David handing over Saul's seven sons to be hanged "to make expiation" (2 Samuel 21)** — Why was this allowed? Likely belongs with the brutal-era pattern (compare Agag), where Scripture records human acts without commending them, but study the context before forming a conclusion.
- **"God incited David to number Israel" vs. "Satan tempted David" (2 Samuel 24 / 1 Chronicles 21:1)** — The two accounts attribute the same act to God and to Satan. *Resolved* — see the grounded entry "God Did What He Allowed — and the Meaning of God's Wrath" in Developing Context Notes below.

### 1 Kings

- **God "sends a lying spirit" (1 Kings 22 — Micaiah)** — God is described as putting a lying spirit in the mouth of Ahab's prophets. *Resolved* — part of the "God did what He allowed" pattern; see the grounded entry "God Did What He Allowed — and the Meaning of God's Wrath" in Developing Context Notes below. *(See also the interpretation notes in `biblical-interpretation.md` on illustrations making one point.)*

### 2 Kings

- **The she-bears mauling the youths (2 Kings 2:23–24)** — After Elisha is jeered, two bears maul forty-two youths. Belongs with the other "irreverence" stories (Uzzah; Nadab and Abihu) and should be studied together rather than explained alone.
- **Fire from heaven on the fifties (2 Kings 1)** — Fire consumes the soldiers sent to seize Elijah. Note the explicit contrast in the Gospels: when the disciples wanted to call down fire "as Elijah did," Jesus refused, "I have not come to destroy but to save." Read this severe story through Jesus (see `biblical-interpretation.md`).

### Deuteronomy

- **Severe laws and curses** — How should the warnings, curses, and severe laws be understood in light of Jesus' gentleness?
- **Divorce laws** — Why would God regulate divorce if Jesus later says “from the beginning it was not so”?
- **Illegitimate children excluded for ten generations** — What context explains such a severe-sounding rule?
- **Strong drink in Deuteronomy 14** — Why would God permit strong drink at a religious gathering if that is not His ideal?
- **Do not boil a kid in its mother's milk** — What context explains this unusual command?

## Developing Context Notes

### "God Did What He Allowed" — and the Meaning of God's Wrath

**Surface problem:**  
Scripture repeatedly says God *did* destructive things that, on closer reading, someone else actually did or that God merely permitted: "God hardened Pharaoh's heart"; "thus God slew Saul" (when Saul fell on his own sword); "God incited David to number Israel" (called Satan's work in 1 Chronicles 21:1); "the LORD has put a lying spirit in the mouth of these prophets" (1 Kings 22). Read flatly, these make God the active author of evil and destruction.

**Proper context:**  
These passages form a single pattern in which God accepts responsibility for what He permits. The clearest key is Job: God did not take Job's family, wealth, and health—He permitted Satan to do it—yet the language can read as though God did it. The same idiom runs through the whole Bible. Behind it stands the great-controversy setting, with a real adversary, so the destruction has a real source other than God even when the text says "God did" it.

Chronicles also defines what God's *wrath* is. 2 Chronicles 36:16 says "the wrath of the LORD rose against His people... till there was no remedy." Wrath here is not God lashing out. It is God's turning away in loving disappointment from those who do not want Him, leaving them to the natural consequences of their own rebellious choice. "No remedy" is the physician who can do nothing more, because the patient will not trust him, take the medicine, or come to be healed.

**What God is doing:**  
God is taking responsibility, as a ruler does, for what happens under His permission—without being the one who actually inflicts the destruction. When He "pours out wrath," He is sorrowfully withdrawing His protective presence from those who have finally refused Him, allowing the inevitable result.

**What this shows about God:**  
God is not the active destroyer the language sometimes suggests. He accepts the blame for what He allows, and His "wrath" is the grief-filled withdrawal that respects a final refusal. This is later stated most plainly in Romans 1 (God "gave them up"), and illustrated most fully at the cross, where Jesus was "given up" and died the death of a sinner crying "why have you forsaken me?"—God's wrath being His giving-up, not His torment.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not read "God slew," "God hardened," or "God sent a lying spirit" as God actively causing evil. Do not define wrath as God losing His temper or inflicting punishment. Gather all such passages together; do not explain any one in isolation.

### The Flood

**Surface problem:**  
God destroys nearly the whole human race, including children and animals, by water. On the surface this looks like the most extreme possible act of an arbitrary, vengeful God.

**Proper context:**  
God's ultimate ideal and final method is not to end lives but to let those who reject Him go, allowing natural consequence to run its course. The Flood must therefore be read as an emergency, not as God's normal way of dealing with sin.

By the time of the Flood, humanity had become so corrupt that "every intent of the thoughts of the heart was only evil continually." Only one family remained who could still hear God at all. The line of communication between God and humanity—and with it any possibility of rescue, of the promised Redeemer, of revealing the truth about God—was about to end entirely. Books like Judges give a window into how unimaginably debased people could become and how much evil God lets pass; the pre-Flood world had passed even that point.

God had already tried the patient, gentle way: years of preaching through Noah while the ark was built, and no one listened.

**What God is doing:**  
This is structurally like Sinai. At Sinai God used thunder, smoke, and terror—not to win love, trust, or obedience (power cannot produce those), but to gain the attention of people too hardened to hear a quiet voice, so He could keep speaking to them. The Flood is the same logic at the largest scale: God acted dramatically to *preserve the channel of communication* with humanity, keeping one family who could still listen, so that He would still have someone to speak to and humanity could be given another start.

**What this shows about God:**  
The Flood is not evidence that God's chosen method is destruction. His ideal is never to end a life. The Flood was an emergency intervention to keep open the possibility of relationship and rescue when humanity was about to self-terminate. Like Moses telling the people at Sinai "do not be afraid," we can look at this awesome act and say it was done to keep the door open and give humanity another chance—not the act of the kind of person Satan accused God of being.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not explain the Flood by "they deserved it," "God had to punish sin," or "God gave them many chances." The point is the specific purpose of this *emergency*: preserving the line of communication with humanity when it was about to be lost, using power not to compel love but to keep the channel open for what God still had to reveal.

### Moses Striking the Rock

**Surface problem:**  
Moses appears to be punished severely for one failure after decades of faithfulness. He is not allowed to enter Canaan, while the constantly grumbling people still receive water.

**Proper context:**  
The people were grumbling for water. God did not tell Moses to rebuke them or make a public scene. He told Moses to speak to the rock and give them water. The intended picture was God's generosity toward undeserving people. Moses instead rebuked them—"Hear now, you rebels"—and struck the rock, making God look angry when God was acting graciously.

Moses also had unique influence. He spoke with God plainly and was trusted by the people as God's representative. That made his public misrepresentation especially damaging.

**What God is doing:**  
God is protecting the truth about His character. He is showing that trust and truthful representation matter deeply, especially from those who know Him well.

**What this shows about God:**  
God takes misrepresentation seriously because trust is central to relationship. But His discipline is not rejection: the larger biblical story shows God still treating Moses as His friend, showing him the land, burying him, and later honoring him.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not reduce this to “Moses disobeyed, so God punished him.” The text points deeper: Moses did not trust God enough to show Him rightly before the people.

### Balaam

**Surface problem:**  
God first tells Balaam not to go, then later lets him go. This can make God appear inconsistent.

**Proper context:**  
Balaam wants his own way. God first says no. Balaam keeps pressing. When God lets him go, He does not abandon him to that choice without warning. The angel blocks the road, the donkey sees what Balaam does not, and Balaam's intended curse is turned into blessing.

**What God is doing:**  
God is respecting Balaam's freedom while continuing to warn and obstruct his destructive path.

**What this shows about God:**  
God may let people have their way, but He does not make destruction easy. He keeps seeking, warning, and placing obstacles in the path of loss.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not say simply “God allowed it because Balaam insisted.” The important point is that God continued working with Balaam even after allowing him to go.

### Do Not Boil a Kid in Its Mother's Milk

**Surface problem:**  
The command can seem arbitrary and strange if read alone.

**Proper context:**  
The command appears in a setting where Israel was surrounded by fertility worship. Records of Canaanite worship include the boiling of a kid in its mother's milk as part of that religious setting. If that is the background, the command is not random. It separates Israel from a practice that would remind them of, and draw them back toward, fertility-cult worship.

**What God is doing:**  
God is protecting the people from practices that would reconnect them with degrading worship around them.

**What this shows about God:**  
God's commands are not arbitrary when read in context. He gives concrete safeguards where people are vulnerable.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not treat the command as a timeless arbitrary test of obedience. The context matters.

### Strong Drink in Deuteronomy 14

**Surface problem:**  
The passage appears to permit buying strong drink for a religious gathering, which seems inconsistent with the broader biblical ideal.

**Proper context:**  
The passage concerns people who live far from the gathering place. Instead of requiring them to transport produce over a long distance, they may convert it to money and come to rejoice before the Lord. The key issue is continued contact and participation, not establishing strong drink as God's ideal.

**What God is doing:**  
God is making it easier for people to come, keep in touch, rejoice, and remain connected to worship rather than stay away.

**What this shows about God:**  
God values relationship and continued contact with people who are not yet at the ideal. He leads them from where they are rather than cutting them off because they are not ready.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not use this passage as a flat code proving that everything mentioned is God's ideal. It must be read in the continuous biblical story.

### The Stoning of Achan (Joshua 7)

**Surface problem:**  
Achan and his household are stoned for taking forbidden plunder. To a modern reader this seems extreme and arbitrary as a punishment.

**Proper context:**  
The story must be read through the moral condition of the people in this period, which the book of Judges makes plain. This was a near-barbarous generation for whom human life was cheap. Stoning was their own ordinary standard of justice, used for far less. At the very start of the conquest the people themselves told Joshua that anyone who disobeyed him should be put to death: "Whoever rebels against your commandment and disobeys your words... shall be put to death" (Joshua 1:18). The grossness of the era is shown elsewhere in the same period (for example the Levite's concubine, Judges 19). The shock for us is the severity of stoning; for them, stoning was the normal and expected response.

**What God is doing:**  
God is operating within the moral framework the people already lived by, meeting them where they were rather than imposing a standard of justice entirely foreign to their understanding.

**What this shows about God:**  
God meets people in the context they actually live in. The form of justice here reflects the harsh world of these people, not a picture of God as bloodthirsty. Read against how cheaply this generation already treated life, the account shows God working within their world, not introducing a cruelty alien to it.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not reduce this to "Achan sinned, so God had to punish him," or "God must punish sin." The key is the contrast between the people's own normal standard (stoning for far less) and how the same act reads to us today.

### Samuel Hewing Agag in Pieces (1 Samuel 15)

**Surface problem:**  
The prophet Samuel personally cuts King Agag to pieces "before the Lord." This looks like religiously-sanctioned brutality and can make God appear bloodthirsty.

**Proper context:**  
This act must be read against the violent era already established in Judges and the conquest, where life was cheap and such killing was normal. Crucially, the text records no word from heaven commending the act—God does not endorse it. This belongs with the same pattern as Achan: the harshness reflects the brutal world of these people, not a picture of what God approves. The Bible truthfully records what people did without commending all of it.

**What God is doing:**  
God is working within a violent generation and allowing their world to be recorded honestly. The absence of any divine commendation is significant.

**What this shows about God:**  
The account reveals the character of the people and the era, not a bloodthirsty God. Not everything done "before the Lord" or by a prophet is endorsed by God; Scripture records human acts faithfully without approving them all.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not assume that because a prophet did it "before the Lord," God commanded or approved the manner of it. The text's silence is the point.

### Sending Away the Foreign Wives (Ezra 10; Nehemiah 13)

**Surface problem:**  
After the return from exile, men who had married foreign (pagan) women were required to send those wives—and their children—away. On the surface this looks cruel: God appears to break up families and abandon women and children.

**Proper context:**  
This must be read as a genuine emergency, not God's pattern for marriage. Throughout the Old Testament, intermarriage with surrounding pagan peoples had repeatedly seduced Israel into idolatry—even Solomon, the wisest man, was turned to the worship of foreign gods by his foreign wives. The returned remnant was small and fragile, the last people on earth still carrying the knowledge of the true God and the line through which the promised Redeemer would come. They were "not that strong"; history had proven they could not live alongside this worship without being absorbed into it.

The recording also stresses that the separation was almost certainly carried out as humanely as possible. The divorce provisions in the law existed precisely to make such separations humane, and it is reasonable to assume the wives and children were sent away with provision and care rather than simple abandonment, even though the record gives few details.

**What God is doing:**  
God is preserving the only people left who still carried His truth, at a moment when absorption into surrounding idolatry would have ended that line entirely. This is the same kind of emergency logic seen in the Flood: a drastic act to keep open the channel through which God could continue to reveal Himself and save.

**What this shows about God:**  
This is severe but not ungodlike. God does not hate or despise these women and children; the wider story shows He loves foreigners and welcomes them (Rahab, Ruth). The separation reflects the desperateness of the emergency—preserving the truth-bearing remnant—not a God who is callous toward families.

**Weak explanations to avoid:**  
Do not explain this by "they shouldn't have married pagans, so they deserved it," or treat it as God endorsing the casual breaking of families. The point is the specific emergency: protecting the survival of the only people still carrying the truth.
