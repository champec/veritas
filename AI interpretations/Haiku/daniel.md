# Daniel: God's Character Revealed

*(This recording covers the book of Daniel in two sessions.)*

## God Runs His Universe in the Open

The Heavenly Court scene (Daniel 7) is the centerpiece of the book's view of God. Thrones are set, the Ancient of Days takes His seat, and **a hundred million beings** watch as the court sits in judgment and the books are opened.

- This is the same kind of "family meeting" seen in Job 1–2 and Zechariah 3 — God openly discussing the affairs of the universe before His creatures, allowing Satan to accuse, and answering with evidence.
- A tyrant would rule in secret; God submits His governance to the scrutiny of His creatures because **love and trust require openness**.
- The books being opened means nothing is hidden — even during the Millennium, creatures may examine the records. God does not say "just trust Me, I'm busy"; He says "come and see."

**What this shows about God:** God governs openly, inviting scrutiny, because He wants trust that is based on evidence rather than blind submission. This is the style of governing that goes against solitary rule, and it is why He is so concerned with His reputation — not vanity, but because the universe's trust depends on what is seen about Him.

---

## God Welcomes Sinners Only When It Is Safe — Never by Forgiveness Alone

The "investigative judgment" is framed not as a fearful audit but as the logical conclusion of God's openness.

- The only question before the Heavenly Council is not "has this person been forgiven?" but **"is this person safe to have around?"**
- Manasseh's record is atrocious, but Christ can read the heart and testify that he is now a new person — safe to live next door to.
- Angels welcome redeemed sinners genuinely, not on command — because they have heard the evidence, even Satan's objections, and seen that it is so.
- Forgiveness alone would not be fair to neighbors (e.g., Isaiah) if the person were still unsafe. God's concern is a new heart and right spirit, not mere pardon.

**What this shows about God:** God's admission of sinners is based on real transformation that can be testified to openly — never on arbitrary pardon or sneaking anyone in. This is an example of the kind of government He runs: open, evidence-based, and concerned with the safety and trust of the whole universe.

---

## God Does Not Force — Even to Fulfill Prophecy

The struggle over Cyrus (Daniel 10) reveals how God works to fulfill predictions without violating freedom.

- God predicted through Jeremiah that after 70 years Cyrus would let the people go.
- But Cyrus was not forced. The text says Gabriel (and then Michael/Christ) came to contend with "the prince of the kingdom of Persia" — understood as Satan putting overwhelming pressure on Cyrus *not* to release them.
- Heaven's intervention was not to coerce Cyrus but to **hold off the adversary** so Cyrus remained free to make his own decision.
- The Holy Spirit was prompting Cyrus — and Cyrus could still turn Him down. God's infinite power was not used to overwhelm a single mortal.

**What this shows about God:** God protects freedom even at the cost of cosmic struggle. He will send His most powerful agents to shield a person from coercion — but He will not force that person's choice.

---

## God Can Foreknow Without Violating Freedom

Daniel is presented as genuine prediction, not history written after the event.

- The book reads naturally as Babylon → Medo-Persia → Greece → Rome (the ram of chapter 8 is explicitly identified as Media-Persia; the bear with one raised shoulder matches it).
- Foreknowledge is shown to be **encouraging, not threatening**: the fulfilled prophecies are meant to build faith *after* they happen (John 13:19; 14:29), showing God is not caught by surprise.
- God's foreknowledge does not cancel freedom — He foreknew Judas would betray, yet washed his feet the night before. Knowing is not controlling.

**What this shows about God:** God knows the future and can predict it in detail, yet never uses that knowledge to override a person's freedom. His foreknowledge is offered as evidence we can trust Him.

---

## God Reminds His People He Is in Control — But Power Is Not How He Hopes to Win

The time prophecies and visions of Daniel address a real doubt: God's people often look like they are losing. The bad appear to be winning. God's followers suffer. It is fair to ask whether He has lost control, lost interest, or lacks the power He once showed.

- God quiets that doubt, sometimes even His most earnest followers need a reminder that He does have power, He is in control, and He does know the future.
- When He says it will work out in the end, fulfilled prophecy strengthens our faith — we look back and see He knew it all along.
- **But power is not the basis on which He hopes to win.** Daniel and his friends, though prisoners, demonstrate that what matters most to God is truth and character over outward might and power. This is why His people often look troubled and why the wicked appear to be winning — God is not competing on the basis of power. He uses reminders of power only to sustain faith, never as the foundation of it.

**What this shows about God:** God is willing to remind His people of His power when their faith wavers, but the way He actually wins the universe is by demonstration of His character. Power is a reassurance, not the basis of trust.

---

## God Meets Power-Worshippers in Their Language (Nebuchadnezzar)

Nebuchadnezzar's arc across chapters 1–4 shows God meeting a power-worshipping king where he was.

- The king only understood force and displays of power. God met him there — the dream, the fiery furnace, the fourth figure in the fire.
- Nebuchadnezzar's first response was still force-based: "anyone who speaks against this God will be torn limb from limb" (Daniel 3:29). Reverence, but not yet conversion.
- After the humbling of chapter 4, the king finally praises God with no decree of force — "all His works are right and His ways are just, and those who walk in pride He is able to abase" (4:37). He has come around.
- The contrast is explicit: Nebuchadnezzar said "worship my god or I'll burn you up" — and the recording points out most Christians believe God says essentially the same thing about hell. The question is raised: **can you force love?**

**What this shows about God:** God adapts His approach to where a person is — even speaking the language of power to a power-worshipper — while patiently working toward genuine, unforced conversion.

---

## God Comes in Human Form (the Son of Man)

Daniel 7:13 — "one like a son of man" comes with the clouds of heaven.

- In Aramaic, "son of man" simply means a human being (Gabriel calls Daniel "son of man" in 8:17). The point is that the supreme figure in the Heavenly Court **looks like a human**.
- Revelation 1 shows the same — the glorified Christ still looks like a human being.
- God does not come in overwhelming majesty and power to coerce obedience. He comes as one of us, persuading humbly. Those who despise that miss Him; those who see it say "that is a God you could trust."

**What this shows about God:** God prefers to persuade as a humble human rather than overwhelm as a sovereign. His willingness to look like one of us is central to His character.

---

## God's People Reflect His Character in Captivity

Daniel and his friends, though prisoners, behave magnificently — and God develops great people even under unfavorable circumstances.

- Daniel's refusal of the king's food was primarily a **religious** refusal to acknowledge Babylon's gods (the food was offered to idols), not a health measure — though health benefited too.
- The "situation ethics" comparison with Paul (Romans 14; 1 Cor. 10) shows unswerving loyalty to principle applied differently as situations change.
- Daniel's courage in the lions' den — praying openly knowing the consequence — and his identification with his people's sin ("we have sinned") in chapter 9.
- Nebuchadnezzar may have wound up a better man than many of the captives he took.

**What this shows about God:** God is not dependent on favorable circumstances to develop people who reflect His character. Even in captivity, His friends demonstrate courage, integrity, and loyalty that witness to who He is.

---

## Notes on What Is NOT Claimed

- The detailed time-prophecy calculations (2300 days, 1260 days, 70 weeks, Daniel 11's king of the North) are treated as **faith-building after fulfillment**, not as the primary message. The recording explicitly cautions against preoccupation with scheduling remaining events.
- The "wrath of God" and end-time fire are flagged as **to be handled carefully when we reach Revelation** — not explained here in isolation.
- The apocryphal Habakkuk/lions' den story is noted as non-canonical and not used as evidence.
