# Ecclesiastes & Song of Solomon: God's Character Revealed

*(Background: these two short books are studied together. **Ecclesiastes** is the memoir of Solomon as a tired old king, looking back on a life he largely wasted—"vanity of vanities, all is vanity." He had every gift, fortune, and pleasure, and found it all empty. **Song of Solomon** is a poem celebrating human love between a man and a woman; like Esther, it never once mentions God. Neither book looks, at first glance, like an obvious place to learn about God's character—and that is exactly why both turn out to be so revealing.)*

---

## God Delights in Love (Song of Solomon)

This is the heart of what the Song of Solomon reveals. Why is a whole book celebrating human love—never even naming God—placed in the very middle of the Bible? Because the God who designed it that way is **warm and personal, not a distant, impersonal deity**.

The story underneath the poem is simple: Solomon had many wives and concubines, most of whom he never chose (they "came with treaties"), but there was one girl he genuinely loved. "She looked terrific to him, and he looked rather good to her, and they told each other so," and they loved being together. The recording presses the question: could the book really be *just* about human love—and if so, why put it in the Bible?

The answer is the character payoff:

- **God designed human love on purpose.** When God planned to create this world, He could have made us any way at all. "He made us male and female, and so designed it that couples would be very much attracted to each other." That He then placed a celebration of that love dead-center in a Bible full of laws, wars, and sacrifices means it "means a great deal to Him"—He *wants* people to feel this way about each other. A God who could put honeymoon feelings into human beings "thought them up first," and is plainly not "an impersonal deity presiding from some distant point."

- **He uses the warmest human relationships to picture His own love.** Throughout Scripture God says, in effect, "I feel about you the way a husband feels about his wife" (Hosea, Isaiah 54, Jeremiah 3, 2 Corinthians 11:2, Ephesians 5) and "the way a good father feels about his children." When even those fail, He reaches for the gentlest picture of all: "As a hen gathers her chicks under her wings, so I have wanted to gather you." Creating us male and female, capable of love and family, was one more of "the many and various ways" He has tried to show how warmly He feels about us.

- **True religion is warm, not cold.** Religion "has often made people rather distant, and cold, and severe and stern," and has even branded this God-given love as evil. Both the cheapening of love into excess *and* the condemning of it as sinful are perversions of a good gift—the adversary "works both sides of the street." The Song of Solomon is meant to be the remedy.

**What this shows about God:**
- God is warm and personal, and He delights in love. He created it, called it good, and uses the dearest human bonds to picture His own feeling for us. *(Recorded as Principle 13 in `broader-concepts.md`.)*

## God Fully Restores the One Who Comes Home (Ecclesiastes)

The most striking thing about Ecclesiastes is not a verse inside it—it is the fact that it exists at all. Solomon wasted his life on a colossal scale; by the record he sank even lower than his father David (he built altars to false gods and even "passed his children through the fire" to Molech). And yet, at the end, he came home—and God honored him by inspiring him to write *another book of the Bible*, numbering him "among the holy men of God moved by the Holy Spirit," and treating him "with dignity and respect."

This is the prodigal son told for real:

- The prodigal's father had already forgiven him and was watching the road long before the boy finished his speech—"the kindness of his father led him to repentance." The returning failure comes back "first class," treated "as if he had always been God's loyal child," handed the ring (which was the father's checkbook—full trust restored).

- Solomon is the living demonstration of that parable, and David is another: David wrote Psalm 51, one of the most precious passages in Scripture, *right after* the worst act of his life. God did not merely tolerate these returned failures—He entrusted them with writing His word.

- The contrast is sobering and is about *us*, not about God: Solomon, Samson, and David came home; Saul and Judas did not. "Jesus looked at Judas the same way He looked at Peter"—same God, same look on His face. Peter repented; Judas did not. Whether we come back is up to us.

**What this shows about God:**
- God is gracious to the worst failures and fully restores the one who returns—even entrusting His word to him. *(This reinforces Principle 6, "God Is Gracious to His Enemies," which already lists Solomon; recorded there rather than as a new principle.)*
- Solomon's life is *evidence*, not merely a claim. "A text is just a statement, a claim or a promise; the history of this thing is demonstration." With the prodigal son it was a parable; with Solomon it was for real. *(This reinforces Principle 10, "God Wins Trust by Demonstration and Evidence.")*

## How the Gospel's Order Works: Forgiveness First, Then Repentance

A key line from the Ecclesiastes study reframes the whole order of salvation: **"I don't believe God forgives us when we repent. I believe we repent when we find God has long ago forgiven us."** Forgiveness is *prior*; discovering it is what melts the heart and turns the sinner home—"the kindness of his father led him to repentance." Solomon and David writing Scripture after their worst failures are the lived proof of this.

*(This is an interpretation point about how the gospel works, not a separate trait of God's character. It is recorded as "God's Forgiveness Comes First—Repentance Is Our Response to It" in `biblical-interpretation.md`.)*

## Interpretation Notes

These two books also teach how to *read* Scripture carefully:

- **Read Ecclesiastes whole—take only the author's final conclusion.** Most of the book is the language of a discouraged old man, not settled doctrine. The classic misuse is plucking "the dead know nothing" (Eccl 9:5) as a proof-text; that line expresses Solomon's gloom, and the book itself points us to its real verdict at "the end of the matter" (12:13). *(Recorded in `biblical-interpretation.md`.)*

- **Take the simplest natural meaning before forcing an allegory (Song of Solomon).** The full "Christ-and-the-church" allegory collapses on its own details (who are the sixty queens and eighty concubines?). The book's plain meaning—human love—itself speaks eloquently of God, and nothing is lost by reading it simply. *(Recorded in `biblical-interpretation.md`.)*

## Summary

Two books that barely seem religious—one a record of regret, the other a love poem that never names God—turn out to reveal a God who is warm, personal, and delighted by love, and who welcomes the worst failure home and restores him completely. Even success and reputation feel empty without Him (Ecclesiastes); and the love He built into us, far from being unspiritual, is one of His clearest pictures of how He feels about us (Song of Solomon).
