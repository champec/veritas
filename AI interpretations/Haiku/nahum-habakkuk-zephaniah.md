# Nahum, Habakkuk & Zephaniah: God's Character Revealed

*(This recording covers Nahum, Habakkuk, and Zephaniah in two sessions.)*

## God's Strong Language Is an Emergency Measure — Not His Preferred Way

These three books use some of the most severe language in Scripture. The recording frames this as **God's emergency measures**.

- "Is that the fearsome Father or the gentle Son talking? ... Jesus said, 'If you've seen me, you've seen the Father.' ... Isn't this the record of Scripture — God will talk like this when the audience calls for it."
- God prefers the still small voice (as with Elijah — wind, earthquake, and fire were emergency measures). He prefers to speak softly, as He did to Moses.
- When God raises His voice, He runs the risk of people serving Him from fear — which He hates, because "the obedience that springs from fear produces the character of a rebel."
- When Jesus came, He almost never talked this way — and received little respect. "If you talk softly, don't count on winning the respect of many people." God is caught: raise His voice and people serve from fear; speak softly and they don't take Him seriously.
- The ideal: a teacher who has order and respect in the room without the children being afraid. "Those are very special people."

**What this shows about God:** God does not like talking in severe language. He uses it only as an emergency measure when people won't listen any other way. His preference is always the still small voice — but He is willing to risk being misunderstood if it means a few people will take Him seriously and then be quiet enough to hear the truth.

## The Just Shall Live by Trust (Habakkuk 2:4)

Habakkuk 2:4 — "the just shall live by his faith" — is the source of Paul's verse in Romans 1. But the meaning is not about legal forgiveness.

- "Faith" here means **trust** — a willingness to wait for God to work out His plan in His own time.
- Habakkuk was puzzled: "Lord, why don't you do something?" God replied: "I am doing something, but you wouldn't believe it if I told you." Habakkuk was willing to wait.
- "If you're right with God — if you know Him, if you're His friend, if you trust Him — you'll be willing to wait."
- This is the same trust Abraham showed: "Abraham trusted God, and God said, 'That's good. That's all I've ever wanted.'"
- Trust doesn't say anything good about us — it says something good about the One we trust. "That's the beauty of emphasizing faith — it's the end of pride."

**What this shows about God:** God is looking for trust, not legal adjustment. Those who are right with Him are marked by a willingness to wait for Him to work out His plan. Trust is the mark of a real relationship with God.

## God Waits Until There Is No Further Hope (Nahum)

Nahum is the counterpart to Jonah. Nineveh repented under Jonah; by Nahum's time, they had regressed and would not respond.

- God waited — that is His character. But finally, when there was no more hope, He let them go.
- "He waited even for the Assyrians. Of course, when finally there was no more hope — read the book of Nahum — He did let them go."
- The strong language of Nahum reflects that this was the end of the line. It was too late for Nineveh.
- God had used Assyria to discipline His people (Isaiah 10:5 — "the rod of my anger"), but Assyria overdid it — "it was in God's mind to discipline, but in the Assyrians' mind to destroy." So God disciplined Assyria in turn.

**What this shows about God:** God waits as long as there is any hope. He only lets go when there is no further response — and even then, the strong language reflects the desperation of the emergency, not God's preferred tone.

## God Did Not Create Babylon to Punish — He Withdrew Protection

A key insight from the discussion: God did not raise up the Chaldeans specifically to punish His people.

- "Did God create a country, strong and mighty and fearful in battle, to teach His people a lesson? No — because of His people's unfaithfulness, because they broke trust with God, He simply said: 'This hand that I have used to protect you all these years — you want to do it on your own.'"
- So long as God defended Israel, the Babylonians had no chance. When Israel broke trust with God, they no longer had His protecting power — and the Babylonians came.
- "It wasn't God creating people to get even with His own people. He simply had to say, 'You want to do it on your own — I can't help you if you insist.'"
- God loved the Chaldeans too and worked with them. "We'll probably see Nebuchadnezzar in the Kingdom."

**What this shows about God:** God does not raise up enemies to punish His people. He simply withdraws His protection when His people insist on going their own way. The destruction that follows is the natural consequence of being without God's protection, not a punishment God sends.

## The Delay Confirms God's Character — We Should Be Proud, Not Embarrassed

Habakkuk and Zephaniah speak directly to Adventists embarrassed by the delay of the second coming.

- "Are we proud of God for waiting? Are we willing to be quiet and give Him time to work out His plan?"
- "If our message is about God instead of about ourselves, we can turn the delay to our advantage. His waiting — we're proud of Him for waiting."
- "The advent message gets stronger every additional year that God waits. Actually, it's so biblical — He's always done it before."
- The signs were right (1780, 1798, 1833, 1844), but the delay is partly our fault — and partly God's mercy. "The unreadiness of God's people is the reason for so long delay."
- We should not use fear of the end to move people to prepare — "the shortness of time is urged as an incentive for us to seek righteousness... This is not the great motive. It savers of selfishness."

**What this shows about God:** The delay is not an embarrassment to God — it is evidence of His character. He waits because He is not willing that any should perish. We should be proud of a God who waits, not ashamed of the delay.

## God Respects Humanity Too Much to Force

A recurring theme across all three books.

- "The one thing that impresses me as I study all of these prophets is the great respect God has for humanity. I think God respects us more than we realize, because He refuses to turn us into robots."
- "I respect you too much to force you to accept my help if you don't want it. I respect you too much to be your God if you don't want me to be."
- God gave people the power of choice and would not tamper with it — even when they were dead wrong.

**What this shows about God:** God's respect for human freedom is so great that He will not force even those who are destroying themselves. He prefers to let people go rather than override their choice.

## Sin Will Not Arise a Second Time (Nahum 1:9)

Nahum 1:9 — "affliction shall not rise up the second time" — is explored in depth.

- Does this mean we won't be free in the hereafter? No — "God's paid such a price to re-establish freedom in His universe, He's not going to take it away. We will be more free than we've ever been."
- The meaning: heaven will be filled with people who know God so well and are such good friends of His that even if the devil were alive and could get to them, they wouldn't believe his lies.
- When Lucifer first rebelled, God could not let him reap the natural consequences immediately — the universe had never seen death and would have served God from fear. So God let it spread, at infinite cost, until the cross demonstrated what sin really does.
- In the hereafter, if someone should entertain rebellious thoughts, God could let them reap the consequences — and the universe would not misunderstand, because the evidence is preserved (Christ's human form, the marks on His body).
- "It's a fail-safe arrangement now. That's why the universe is truly secure against apostasy and defection for eternity."

**What this shows about God:** God has made the universe secure not by taking away freedom but by demonstrating the truth so thoroughly that no one will ever be deceived again. The evidence — preserved in Christ's human form — makes rebellion impossible to spread a second time.

## God's People Misunderstood Discipline for Destruction (Micah 4:11–12)

Though this was left over from Micah, it connects directly to Nahum/Habakkuk.

- "Now many nations are assembled against you, saying, 'Let her be profaned...' But they do not know the thoughts of the Lord; they do not understand His plan."
- The nations thought God was destroying His people. In reality, He was disciplining those He loves.
- "Whom the Lord loves He disciplines" — the nations misunderstood discipline for destruction.
- The Assyrians didn't understand God's plan either — they thought they were conquering, but God was using them as a tool, and then would discipline them in turn.

**What this shows about God:** God's discipline is not destruction. Those who misunderstand God's plan see only ruin; those who trust Him see that He is still working, even through severe circumstances, to save His people.

---

## God Ends With Restoration — Even After the Most Severe Warnings

Like Amos and the other prophets, Zephaniah ends with a picture of restoration.

- "The Lord will take delight in you; in His love He will give you new life. He will sing and be joyful over you, as joyful as people at a festival." (Zephaniah 3:17)
- "I will bring your scattered people home; I will even make you famous throughout the world and make you prosperous once again."
- "Some of the prophets who speak the most severe language against sin then turn around and have the most brilliant descriptions of the good things that are to come."

**What this shows about God:** God's final word is always restoration. Even after the most severe warnings, His purpose is to rebuild, restore, and rejoice over His people. He does not leave His people in ruin.

## Notes on What Is NOT Claimed

- The dating of these prophets (Nahum ~640 BC, Habakkuk ~630 BC, Zephaniah ~639–608 BC) is acknowledged as approximate.
- The vivid imagery of Nahum 2 (chariots, torches, lightning) is left open — whether literal or symbolic, "the devastation coming is clear."
- The discussion of whether the "generation" of the signs means the Jewish people or the people who saw the signs is left as one of multiple interpretations.
