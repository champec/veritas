# Why Jesus Had to Die

Ask most Christians why Jesus died and the answer comes quickly: to pay for our sins. God's justice demanded punishment. Sin had to be punished. Jesus absorbed that punishment in our place so that God could forgive us. The logic feels airtight, the language feels ancient, and the whole framework feels simply like what the Bible teaches.

It is not ancient. It is not self-evidently what the Bible teaches. And the language, examined carefully, turns out to be carrying assumptions that were imported into Christian theology at a specific moment in history, by a specific person, for reasons that had more to do with the social order of eleventh-century Europe than with the text of Paul's letter to the Romans.

That person was Anselm of Canterbury.

---

## Where the theory came from

In 1098, Anselm published a work called *Cur Deus Homo*, which translates as *Why God Became Man*. It is a remarkable piece of reasoning, cast as a dialogue, in which Anselm sets out to explain the necessity of the incarnation and the cross. The framework he used was drawn entirely from the world he inhabited: feudal society, with its governing logic of honour, debt, and satisfaction.

In that world, every wrong was understood as an offence against someone's honour. The severity of the offence was measured not by the act itself but by the rank of the person offended. To insult a peasant was a minor matter. To insult a nobleman required a proportionate act of reparation. To offend the king demanded satisfaction of a kind that matched the king's dignity.

Anselm mapped this directly onto sin. Sin, he argued, is an offence against the infinite honour of God. The debt it creates is therefore infinite. Humans, being finite, cannot pay it. But the debt must be paid, because God's honour cannot simply be set aside. If God forgave without satisfaction, he would be a God who let wrongs go without consequence, which would be beneath him. Therefore a being who was both human (and so could represent humanity) and divine (and so could offer infinite satisfaction) was required. Hence the incarnation. Hence the cross.

The argument is elegant. It is also entirely built on a concept, the feudal logic of honour and satisfaction, that has no foothold in the biblical text. The word "satisfaction" does not appear in Romans. The idea that God's honour creates a debt that must be repaid before forgiveness can occur is not in Paul. It is in the social imagination of a Norman archbishop working four decades after the conquest of England.

---

## How Calvin sharpened it into punishment

Anselm's framework was picked up and significantly recast by John Calvin in the sixteenth century. Calvin was a lawyer before he was a theologian, and he translated Anselm's categories into forensic ones. The currency shifted from honour to law. The problem was no longer a debt to God's dignity but a violation of God's law, and the law demanded punishment.

In this version, God is primarily a judge. His justice requires that wrongdoing be punished. Since every human being has sinned, every human being is under sentence. Christ, by dying, absorbed that sentence. God can now acquit the guilty because the punishment has already been executed on someone else.

This became known as Penal Substitutionary Atonement, and it has dominated Western Protestant theology ever since. It is the version most people encounter when they first hear an explanation of the cross. It sits in hymns, in sermons, in evangelistic frameworks. It is so pervasive that many people assume it is simply the biblical position, rather than a sixteenth-century legal construction built on an eleventh-century feudal construction.

---

## What the framework cannot explain

The problems with this theory are not minor. They arise from the biblical text itself.

Begin with one of Jesus's most famous parables. In Luke 15, a son demands his inheritance, leaves home, wastes everything, and returns in desperation. His father sees him from a distance, runs to meet him, and throws his arms around him before a single word of apology has been spoken. There is no punishment. There is no payment. There is no requirement that someone absorb the consequences before forgiveness can be extended. The father simply forgives, because he loves his son and his son has come home.

Jesus told that story to describe what God is like. If the penal framework were correct, the story is at best incomplete and at worst deeply misleading. It describes a God who forgives freely, on the spot, the moment the conditions of the heart are in place. The framework that requires punishment before forgiveness cannot accommodate that father without significant qualification.

The problem runs deeper than one parable. Throughout the Gospel accounts, Jesus forgives people's sins with no reference to his approaching death. He says to the paralysed man lowered through a roof: your sins are forgiven. He says it to a woman caught in adultery. He says it to a tax collector at a dinner table. He offers forgiveness freely, personally, immediately. If forgiveness required the cross to occur first, none of these instances are coherent. Jesus was either forgiving on credit or the cross was not what made forgiveness possible.

Then there is the language of Genesis itself. When God warns against eating from the tree, the words are: "you will surely die." Not: I will punish you. Not: I will kill you. Not: the law will require your execution. The statement is a description of consequence, not a threat of punishment. It is the same kind of statement as: if you step off this cliff, you will fall. The falling is not the cliff's punishment. It is simply what happens when you step off it. God was describing what sin does to a creature that is built for relationship with him: it severs the connection, and severing the connection from the source of life is what death is.

The penal framework requires God's own declaration to be a threat of execution rather than a description of consequence. That reading is not forced on us by the text. It is imported into it.

---

## What Paul actually wrote

Romans 3:25 is the passage most often cited in support of the penal theory. It is worth reading what Paul actually wrote.

The Greek word translated as "sacrifice of atonement" or "propitiation" is *hilasterion*, the same word used in the Greek Old Testament for the mercy seat, the lid of the ark of the covenant where blood was sprinkled on the Day of Atonement. Paul is saying God set Christ forward as that meeting point between holiness and mercy.

The reason given for this demonstration is stated in a phrase that most English translations soften: *dia ten paresin ton progegonoton hamartematon*, "because of the passing over of the previously committed sins." The key word is *paresin*, which is not the ordinary Greek word for forgiveness. That word is *aphesin*. Paul chose a different word, one that means a deferral, a passing over, a suspension of consequence. He is not saying God forgave OT sins. He is saying God passed over them, held them in abeyance, did not bring them to their full reckoning.

The sentence says God demonstrated his righteousness because he had been passing over sins. The subject of the problem is not sinners needing a mechanism of forgiveness. The subject is God's own righteousness being in question. His *dikaiosyne*, his integrity and truthfulness, needed demonstrating. The passing over of sins across all of human history had raised a serious question about whether God meant what he said.

That is the problem Paul identifies. The cross is the answer to that problem.

---

## The charge that required an answer

To understand the problem, go back to the beginning. When God said that sin leads to death, a counter-claim was immediately lodged: you will not die. God's word was directly challenged. And then, from the perspective of the challenger, things appeared to go rather well. The first people to sin did not die immediately. God, in his mercy, stayed with them. He clothed them. He set a long redemptive project in motion. Every generation of human life that passed without the ultimate consequence arriving was another apparent point in favour of the original lie.

God's forbearance, which came from love, was being read as evidence of dishonesty or weakness. The patience was real. The mercy was real. But it was accumulating an appearance problem that required resolution.

God does not answer challenges with counter-arguments. The book of Job is the precedent. When the character of God and the nature of his relationship with humanity were publicly contested, God did not issue a rebuttal. He permitted a demonstration. The truth was established through what could be seen, not through what could be asserted. Job's life became the evidence.

The cross is the same logic, at a scale Job was only previewing.

When Jesus cries from the cross, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?", he is experiencing exactly what God described in Eden: complete separation from the source of life. Not execution by an outraged deity. The forsaking. The withdrawing of presence that is what death, in its ultimate form, actually is. The universe that had been watching millennia of apparent contradiction between God's words and God's actions saw the truth demonstrated in real time. Sin leads to death. The description was accurate. The warning was real. The serpent's claim was false.

The cross does not demonstrate that God needed to be satisfied before he could love us. It demonstrates that God told the truth and can be trusted.

---

## Why the difference matters

This is not a dispute over terminology. It changes everything about what kind of God is being described.

In the penal framework, God is fundamentally a judge whose legal requirements must be met before his love can operate. His forgiveness is downstream of a transaction. The cross is what made it possible for him to feel kindly towards us. Before the payment, his wrath stood between him and us. This God looks generous because he arranged for the payment himself, but the structure still requires the payment. His love, in this account, is conditional on justice being served.

In the account the text actually offers, God is a God whose love was never in question. His forbearance throughout history was love. His sending of his son was love. The cross was not the price of his affection. It was the cost of telling the truth in a way the whole universe could see, under conditions where a lie had been running for a very long time.

The prodigal son's father ran towards his child. He was not running towards him because a debt had been cleared. He was running because his son had come home. That is the God the cross reveals. Not a God who needed to be paid. A God who needed to be believed.

What the cross restores is the rational ground for trust. The oldest wound in human experience is not guilt, it is doubt: the suspicion, planted and cultivated, that God's word cannot be relied upon, that his character is something other than what he claims. The cross answers that doubt with evidence that cannot be argued away. It places the demonstration in history, in a body, in a death, in a resurrection that shows what life looks like when the separation is ended.

When that evidence is received, and trust begins to form, the actual work of restoration can begin. Not because a transaction was completed. Because a relationship was repaired.

That is what Paul is saying in Romans 3:25. God demonstrated his righteousness. Not satisfied his wrath. Demonstrated his righteousness. The word is *endeixin*: a public showing, a proof offered to an audience. It presupposes a contested claim and an observing universe waiting to see how it is resolved.

The claim was contested in Eden. The demonstration came at Calvary. The case is closed.
