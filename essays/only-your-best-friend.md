# Only Your Best Friend Would Show You

## Normal

Betty thought Ron was perfect. That was the problem.

He had been everything on their first date: flowers, an opened door, complete attention. He was good-looking in a way that felt like a bonus rather than the point. She walked home that evening thinking, for the first time in a long time, that the whole thing might actually work out. The modern dating scene is not kind. There are too many people, too many options, too little reason for anyone to mean what they say. Ron seemed to mean it.

He did not mean it.

Ron was seeing several women at the same time. His friends knew. Some of the people at their university knew. They watched him with Betty and said nothing, for the usual reasons: it was none of their business, she would find out eventually, she probably would not believe them anyway, and Ron had a way of remembering who crossed him. Some of them felt sorry for her. Most of them used the whole situation as entertainment. Sympathy without action is not really sympathy. It is just a feeling people have while doing nothing.

Then there was Casey.

Casey was Betty's best friend, and when she found out, she was furious. Not the dramatic, gossipy kind of furious, but the quiet, determined kind that comes from genuinely caring about someone. She confronted the mutual friend who had known longest and done nothing. Why didn't you say anything? The answer came back: you know how she is. She'll think we're exaggerating. She'll think we're jealous. We tried once. It's not worth the trouble.

Casey went straight to Betty.

Betty dismissed it. She did not do so cruelly; she just could not make the picture fit. Ron was attentive, affectionate, consistent. He called when he said he would. He remembered small things. The story Casey was telling did not match the person she thought she knew. You must be mistaken, she said. You must be jealous. I think you should leave.

Casey stood at a crossroads. She could leave it there. Betty was an adult. She had been given the information, and what she did with it was her own business. Perhaps the kindest thing really was to step back and let her make her own choices.

But Casey thought about what was coming. Betty was not simply dating Ron: she was falling. She was introducing him to her family. She was rearranging her life around him, turning down shifts, spending money on gifts, considering whether she might move cities to close the distance. Every week that passed was another week of her life built on something false. The longer it went, the worse the eventual reckoning. And Betty would know, when it finally came, that her friends had watched it happen. That they had known and said nothing. That would be its own kind of wound.

Casey decided that being hated in the short term was worth the risk.

She joined a dating app. Found Ron's profile. Began talking to him. Over the following weeks she gathered screenshots, recorded voice notes, documented what he was doing and who he was doing it with. Then she went back to Betty, and this time she came with evidence. Not accusations: evidence. I have proof, she said. If you want to see it, here it is. I can call him right now, while you listen. You can hear him with your own ears.

Betty looked at the evidence. She was shattered.

Her first instinct was to blame Casey. She had gone looking for this. She had probably encouraged him. She cast Casey out entirely. But the truth, once shown, does not go away on command. It sat there, patient, waiting. Over time, Betty was forced to do what she had refused to do immediately: look at it. When she did, she contacted Casey and apologised.

Only your best friend would do what Casey did. The bystanders had their reasons, and every one of those reasons was understandable. It cost nothing to say nothing. Casey paid a price. She risked the friendship she valued most, spent weeks gathering evidence she would rather not have found, and walked back into a room where she had already been dismissed once. She did it because she loved Betty more than she feared losing her.

---

There is a warning that has been in question for as long as human memory goes back. The original claim was straightforward: certain choices lead to death, and not the ordinary kind. The counter-claim arrived almost immediately: you will not really die. That counter-claim has been running ever since, in one form or another. It is comfortable. It requires nothing. It asks only that the warning be set aside.

What the long trajectory of that choice has produced in us is honest if not flattering. We are creatures shaped, at least in part, by the logic of survival: oriented toward ourselves first, alert to threat and advantage, calculating cost before kindness. A newborn cries not for its parents but for its own needs. The animal kingdom shows the same principle without restraint. This is not the whole picture of what we are, but it is a true part of it.

The other part shows itself in the moments that do not fit the survival logic at all: the person who runs toward danger when everyone else is running away, the impulse to help a stranger before any calculation of cost, the capacity to feel someone else's pain as though it were one's own. These glimpses do not fit the model because they were not built by it. They point toward something that was not entirely lost.

Into this situation, at a specific moment in history, something happened that was neither an assertion nor an argument. It was a demonstration. The cross was the cost of showing rather than merely saying. What it showed was not simple, and it is worth being specific.

It confirmed that the warning was real. There is a death beyond ordinary dying, a final separation, and the cross made the reality of it visible rather than leaving it as a claim to be accepted or dismissed. It showed what that death actually is: not execution at the hands of an outraged power, but the experience of being given up. Jesus cried out the question that gives it its full weight: *My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?* The forsaking is the thing. God's role in the death that sin leads to is not the role of the one who strikes down. It is the role of the one who must finally, at the limit of every other option, let go.

It also showed what love can and cannot do. Genuine love cannot compel a choice. There comes a point at which it must release what refuses to be held, and that releasing is itself an act of love, not a failure of it. The grief in it is real. The cross does not conceal that.

What does all of this say about the one who did it? He told the truth when the easier thing would have been silence or something more comfortable. He went further than any reasonable expectation could have demanded. He paid a price that cannot be explained by anything other than genuine care for the people He was trying to reach. He did not describe the situation from a safe distance; He entered it, demonstrated it from the inside, and left the evidence where it could be seen.

These are not the actions of someone indifferent to us. They are not the actions of someone after compliance. They are the actions of a friend.

Only your best friend would show you. The evidence is there. The cost has been paid. The choice, as it has always been, remains yours.
