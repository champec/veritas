# Why Did Jesus Have to Die?

There is a question buried inside the story of salvation that most people never think to ask. Not *could* God forgive us — but *why did it cost anything at all?* If God is sovereign, if he is love, if forgiveness is simply his nature, why does the cross feel necessary? Why not just forgive and move on?

Paul gives us the answer, but it requires following his argument carefully.

---

In Romans 3, Paul makes a statement that sounds almost strange: *"Let God be true though every man a liar."* And a few lines later: *"that you may be justified in your words and prevail when you are judged."* These are courtroom words. Paul is describing a universe in which God himself is on trial — and in which the verdict genuinely matters.

This is not a minor legal technicality. The question on the table is whether God's word can be trusted. Whether what he says is actually true. Whether the being at the center of everything is reliable.

And that question was raised early.

In the garden, God said plainly: *if you go this way, you will die.* The serpent said just as plainly: *you will not die.* That was the original dispute — not about fruit, not even about obedience, but about whether God tells the truth. Satan's assault has never really changed. It has always been a case built against God's credibility.

For thousands of years after, something seemed off. People sinned. They were not struck down. Generation after generation lived, broke faith, and died of ordinary causes. God warned, sent prophets, pleaded — but he did not execute the sentence he had announced. Paul calls this, strikingly, a *passing over of former sins.* Not forgiveness. Something more like a case left unresolved.

---

Why does it matter that the case gets resolved?

Because God is not merely a rule-maker whose penalties need enforcing. He is the fixed point by which everything in the universe orients itself. The north star does not shine for its own sake — it shines so that everything lost can find its way. A God whose word bends, whose warnings are eventually revealed to be empty, is not a God anyone can truly navigate by. And a God we cannot truly navigate by is one we cannot truly trust. And a God we cannot truly trust is one we cannot freely, wholeheartedly, give ourselves to.

This is not about God protecting his pride. It is about preserving the only relationship that can actually save us. Real trust requires a real object. Faith in a God whose word has been shown to be unreliable is not faith — it is hope in the dark.

So when Paul says God demonstrated his righteousness at the cross, he means something precise. He means: *God proved that he was right.* He was right about sin. He was right about death. He was right about what it means to be abandoned by the source of all life.

---

But here is what makes the demonstration so extraordinary.

God did not prove he was right by letting us die.

The theme of God *giving up* runs through scripture like a quiet, aching thread. In Hosea, God cries out — *how can I give you up? How can I let you go?* — the words of someone who loves and cannot bring himself to release what he loves, even when it keeps pulling away. In Romans 1, Paul describes three moments where God *gave them up* — to their desires, to their passions, to their darkened minds. And each time, the thing he gave them up to was not a punishment he imposed. It was simply reality, unfiltered. The natural shape of life when the one who is Life itself withdraws.

God never fully gave up on humanity in that old age. That was, precisely, the passing over. He kept reaching, kept calling, kept sending. He did not give them up the way he would have been justified in doing. And so death, in its fullest sense — the complete forsakenness, the total abandonment — was never demonstrated. The serpent's case remained technically open.

The cross closes it.

On the cross, for the first and only time, God gives someone up completely. *My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?* That is not poetry. That is the moment God lets go — and the one he lets go of dies. Not from a wound, not from exhaustion alone, but from forsakenness. The warning in the garden finally falls in full force, and it falls on Christ. God was right. He has always been right. And now the universe knows it.

---

But — and this is everything — Christ is not left there.

The resurrection is God's counter-move. It says: *sin leads to death, I have demonstrated that, the truth is established, the case is closed* — and now, because I have upheld my own word, I can do what I have always wanted to do. I can bring people back. I can make them right. I can justify them.

Paul says this righteousness is received by faith — by those who *believe him*. And this makes a particular kind of sense now. Faith is not merely intellectual agreement. It is the restoration of the relationship that sin broke — a relationship of trust, of knowing who God is, of being willing to be known by him in return. Those who believe him are the ones who benefit from the demonstration, because they are the ones who come back into communion with the being who demonstrated it.

The prodigal son's father did not wait on the porch with a list of consequences to settle before the embrace. He ran. He had already forgiven long before the boy appeared on the road. But the boy had to turn around and come home. That turning — that return — is what faith looks like in practice.

---

And for those who will not turn?

God fights. He does not give up easily. The whole history of Israel is God refusing to give up on people who kept walking away. He sends Hosea to marry an unfaithful woman as a living parable of his own heart. He sends prophet after prophet. He weeps over Jerusalem. The fight in God between love and the grief of letting go is one of the most human things in all of scripture — because love does not release what it loves without a cost.

But love also knows when to let go.

If someone insists on living as though God does not exist, insists on refusing the relationship, insists on their own way — the most loving thing God can do, finally, is to respect that choice. To let them have the world they chose. To give them up, as he gave people up in Romans 1 — not in anger, but in sorrow, the way a father watches a son walk out the door knowing what waits for him outside.

This is not punishment. It is consequence. It is the shape of a universe built around a being who will not force himself on anyone.

---

So why did Jesus have to die?

He did not have to. We could have died. The sentence was ours. But God, who fights before he lets go, chose to absorb the consequence himself — to let the forsakenness land on Christ, to demonstrate in his own person that his word was true, to close the serpent's case once and for all, and to do it in a way that left the door open for every human being who had ever lived.

He was right about death. He proved it. And having proved it, he is now able, without any contradiction, to offer life — to anyone willing to come home.

*That you may be justified in your words, and prevail when you are judged.*

He prevailed. And because he did, so can we.
